# Project Stages — concept → construction
> The spine of a project. Use it to (a) know what deliverable is appropriate NOW, and (b) tell the user
> when they're asking for something out of sequence. Stage names vary by market (RIBA 0–7 · AIA
> SD/DD/CD) — the sequence doesn't. **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

---

## Naming — map it once, then forget the labels

| Here | RIBA (UK) | AIA / US | Common EU / FR |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 Brief / feasibility | 0 Strategic Definition · 1 Preparation & Brief | Pre-Design / Programming | Études préalables · Faisabilité |
| 1 Concept | 2 Concept Design | Conceptual Design | Esquisse (ESQ) |
| 2 Schematic (SD) | 2→3 (upper Concept / early Spatial Coordination) | Schematic Design | APS (avant-projet sommaire) |
| 3 Design development (DD) | 3 Spatial Coordination | Design Development | APD (avant-projet définitif) |
| 4 Construction docs (CD) | 4 Technical Design | Construction Documents | PRO / DCE · permit set |
| 5 Tender & construction | 5 Manufacturing & Construction | Bidding + CA (contract admin) | ACT + DET |
| 6 Handover & in-use | 6 Handover · 7 Use | Closeout / Post-occupancy | AOR + GPA |

The labels argue; the logic doesn't. Every project moves from *is it possible* → *what is it* →
*how is it organised* → *how is it built*. Don't correct a user's vocabulary — read their **artefacts**
(see "Reading the user's stage") and place them on this spine yourself.

---

## Stage 0 — Brief / feasibility

**You have:** a site (survey, plot boundary, cadastre, photos, or just an address), a client brief in
words, a budget number, and constraints (zoning, setbacks, height limit, FAR/plot ratio, access,
existing structures, orientation, climate).

**You produce:** site analysis (sun, wind, noise, access, views, topography), envelope-of-the-possible
massing (what zoning *permits*, not what you *want*), an indicative area schedule (GFA / NIA / efficiency
target), a cost band (± 30 %, not a budget), and a go/no-go note.

**The question this stage answers:** *Can this brief fit on this site, for this money, under these rules?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** the **brief itself** — the schedule of accommodation and the target
GFA. Adding a whole programme later (an extra floor, a second use, +2 000 m²) is a new project, not a
change. Also locked in practice: the **site**. Everything else is still fluid.

**What this skill can do here:** §29 urban/site + **§07 diagram style** and §35 urban graphics for site
analysis diagrams (sun path, access, noise, view cones); **render type 5** (site analysis diagram) and
**type 6** (concept massing) for a permitted-envelope massing study; **§48** floor-plan design &
calculation for a first area schedule; **§56** cost & buildability for a cost band; **§55** building code
& safety check to test the envelope against setbacks/height; **§29** for masterplan-scale briefs.

**Common mistake:** producing a beautiful massing render before anyone has confirmed the plot ratio.
A gorgeous scheme that breaches the height limit is worth nothing. Test the constraints first, then
draw.

## Stage 1 — Concept design

**You have:** an agreed brief, an area target, site analysis, a budget band.

**You produce:** concept diagrams (the idea in one drawing), massing options (2–4, not 12), the **parti**
(the single organising idea — a bar, a courtyard, a stacked plinth, a carved block), precedent board /
moodboard, loose sketch plans, one or two concept renders, an atmosphere.

**The question this stage answers:** *What **is** this building?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** the **parti and the massing** — building footprint, rough height,
number of storeys, orientation, and where the front door is. You can still move a wall. You cannot
still "try it as a courtyard instead" once SD starts without throwing away SD.

**What this skill can do here:** **§07** diagram style + **§52** diagram proposal system for concept and
parti diagrams; **§38** concept illustration & annotated isometric; **§26 moodboard system** + §33
materials moodboard + **§43** reference library for precedent/atmosphere; **render type 6** (concept
massing) and **type 12** (client presentation image); §14 architectural styles + §02 lighting for the
character of the concept render; **§81** style-reference matching if they bring someone else's look
(their architecture, the reference's *look* — never the reference's building); **§80** visual consistency
if the concept needs a coherent set.

**DO NOT here:** dimension anything precisely, draw a structural grid you'll defend, specify a product,
or produce a detail. A concept render that implies a resolved mullion is a lie you will pay for at DD.

**Common mistake:** over-resolving. A 4K photoreal render at concept freezes decisions nobody has made
yet — the client falls in love with a curtain wall that the budget will delete at DD. Keep concept
outputs legibly *concept*: diagram, massing, atmosphere.

## Stage 2 — Schematic design (SD)

**You have:** an approved concept + parti, brief, budget. Engineers (structure, MEP) are now engaged.

**You produce:** developed plans / sections / elevations at a real scale; the **structural grid** agreed
with the engineer; the **core** located (stairs, lifts, risers, WCs); a refined area schedule with
efficiency (NIA/GFA) checked against target; circulation and egress logic; materials **direction** (a
palette, not a spec); façade parti (solid-to-void, rhythm, depth); key presentation renders.

**The question this stage answers:** *How is the building organised — grid, core, circulation, envelope?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** the **structural grid** and the **core**. This is the sharpest lock in
the whole project. Moving a column line or relocating a stair core after SD is a **redesign, not a
revision** — it invalidates the plans, the egress calc, the riser strategy, the area schedule and the
engineer's scheme in one move. Also locked: storey count, gross footprint, and the egress strategy.

**What this skill can do here:** **§40 floor plan system** + **§48** plan design & calculation (test the
grid against the plan, recompute areas/efficiency); **§57 structural logic review** (does the grid
actually land — spans, transfers, cantilevers, column-free spans that aren't); **§55** code & safety
(egress distances, stair count, corridor widths); **§64** façade composition analysis; **§51** material
/ colour recommendation for the palette; **§26/§33/§46** moodboards & materials board; **render types
1, 2, 7, 12** (exterior, interior, façade, presentation); **§39** section perspective to prove the
section works; **§28** axonometric to show the grid + core relationship; **§80** for a consistent SD
render set.

**Common mistake:** rendering the façade in loving detail while the core is still "roughly there".
The core is the thing that will bite you. Also: signing off an area schedule that quietly assumes 90 %
efficiency — check it with §48 before it becomes a promise.

## Stage 3 — Design development (DD)

**You have:** a locked grid and core, an agreed SD package, engaged engineers, a cost plan.

**You produce:** coordinated plans (arch + structure + MEP on the same geometry); **RCPs** (reflected
ceiling plans); developed sections; **key details** (typical wall build-up, parapet, sill, floor
junction) — *typical*, not every one; the **façade strategy** (system, build-up, thermal line,
fixing logic, thickness); **MEP zones and risers agreed** (plant location, riser sizes, ceiling void
depth); outline specification; FF&E direction; developed lighting design.

**The question this stage answers:** *How does it actually go together, and does everything fit?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** **floor-to-floor height.** This is the DD lock and the most
expensive one to break: it is the resolved sum of structural depth + services zone + ceiling void +
clear head height. Change it later and you change the structure, the façade panelisation, the stair
rises, the lift shaft, the rider on the height limit — and the whole envelope re-panelises. Also locked
by end of DD: the **façade system** (unitised vs stick vs rainscreen vs load-bearing masonry — a
commercial and structural decision, not a finish), **riser sizes and plant footprint**, and the
**ceiling void depth**. Materials are now specified in kind, not just in mood.

**What this skill can do here:** **§30 technical drawings** + **§31 key drawing elements** (draw plans,
sections and elevations that read as drawings, not pictures); **§59 construction details** and **§68
detailing advisor** for the typical details and the wall build-up; **§40/§48** for coordinated plans and
final area take-off; **§49** furniture, FF&E & space planning; **§50** lighting design → RCP;
**§47** PBR / **§10** material library and **§46** materials board for the spec-grade palette; **§27**
floor decor & finishes; **§69** sustainability (thermal line, U-values, shading — decide it here, not at
CD); **§57** structural logic review against the coordinated section; **§39** section perspective and
**render type 10** (construction-style technical visual) to *show* the build-up; **§54** render variation
for material options against a fixed camera; **§42** design review before freezing.

**Common mistake:** treating DD as "SD with more render passes". DD is where you find out the duct
doesn't fit in the ceiling void and the floor-to-floor is 150 mm short. If nothing painful surfaced at
DD, DD didn't happen — it just got deferred to site, where it costs 10×.

## Stage 4 — Construction documentation / technical design (CD)

**You have:** a fully coordinated DD set, a locked section, an agreed façade system, a cost plan that
closes.

**You produce:** the full drawing set (general arrangement plans, sections, elevations, enlarged plans,
core plans); construction details at 1:5 / 1:10 / 1:20; **schedules** — door, window, finish, ironmongery,
sanitary; the full specification (performance and/or prescriptive, by trade section); a coordinated
federated model (clash-detected, not merely merged); and the permit / tender package with the
statutory drawings.

**The question this stage answers:** *Can a contractor build this, and price it, without asking me?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** **everything.** By issue, the design is a contract document. After
this, change happens through variation orders and the client pays for it in money and programme.
Specifically locked: every dimension, every junction, every product, every tag. The one thing still
genuinely open is contractor-designed portions (the shop-drawing scope you deliberately left as
performance spec) — and even that is bounded by the spec.

**What this skill can do here:** **§30 technical drawings** and **§31 key drawing elements** (line
weights, tags, hatching, north point, scale bar, title block conventions); **§59 construction details**
+ **§68** detailing advisor for the detail set; **§40/§48** for GA plans and schedules of areas; **§49**
for FF&E and door/window schedules; **§55** code & safety check on the permit set; **§69** for the
performance clauses; **§71** quality assurance and **§72** second opinion as a set-review pass;
**render type 10** for construction-style explanatory visuals; **§34/§67** for the permit/tender
presentation board. Note the honest limit: this skill *draws and reasons about* technical drawings —
it does not replace a stamped CAD/BIM set. It is a check, a communicator, and an author of intent.

**Common mistake:** producing more renders. Nobody builds from a render. If the client still needs
persuading at CD, something upstream failed. Second mistake: details that contradict the schedules —
run the QA pass (§71) across drawings + schedules + spec, because the three drift apart silently.

## Stage 5 — Tender & construction

**You have:** an issued tender/permit package, a contractor, a site.

**You produce:** RFI responses (fast, drawn, unambiguous); shop-drawing / contractor-submittal review
(reject, comment, or approve — and say which); site instructions and variation impact notes; sample and
mock-up sign-off; as-built markups as things move.

**The question this stage answers:** *Is what's being built what was designed — and where it isn't, what's
the fix?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** the **building.** It exists. Concrete is honest. Changes are now
priced in demolition.

**What this skill can do here:** **§59/§68** to answer a detail RFI with a drawn build-up rather than a
paragraph; **§30/§31** for a clarification sketch that reads as a drawing; **render type 11**
(before & after) and **§08** transformations to compare as-designed vs as-installed from a site photo;
**§42/§71/§72** to review a contractor's proposed substitution against design intent; **§46** materials
board for sample sign-off. **Do not** use this skill to invent a detail on site — flag the geometry
question, don't render a plausible answer over it.

**Common mistake:** answering an RFI with a render. Answer with a dimensioned sketch. Second mistake:
approving a "value-engineered" substitution on look alone — check §69 (performance) and §57
(structure), because the cheap panel usually loses the thermal break or the fixing depth.

---

## Stage 6 — Handover & in-use

**You have:** a finished building, a contractor's as-built set, commissioning data.

**You produce:** the as-built record set, O&M manuals / building manual, warranties, a **POE**
(post-occupancy evaluation) — measured energy vs modelled, occupant satisfaction, what the plan actually
does under use — and lessons fed back to Stage 0 of the next project.

**The question this stage answers:** *Did it do what we said it would?*

**Locked by the end of this stage:** the **record** — the as-built model/drawings become the truth
source for every future fit-out, refurb and disposal. A wrong as-built is a landmine for the next
architect.

**What this skill can do here:** **§70** building life cycle; **§69** sustainability (modelled vs
measured); **§65** space-experience simulation and **§42** design review as the POE lens; **§66**
photography director for the completed-building shoot; **§34/§67** for the case-study board; **§41**
marketing set and **§82** delivery sets for publication; **§62** storytelling for the project narrative.

**Common mistake:** skipping POE because the project is "done". The POE is the only stage that makes
the *next* project better — it's the cheapest design tool you own.

## Reading the user's stage

You will rarely be told the stage. Read the artefact.

| What they send | Almost certainly | Tell by |
|---|---|---|
| Napkin / hand sketch, no dimensions | Stage 1 concept | No scale, no grid, walls are single lines |
| Mood images, precedent photos, "a vibe" | Stage 1 (or Stage 0) | No geometry at all |
| Massing model screenshot (SketchUp, grey boxes) | Stage 0–1 | Volumes only, no interior |
| Plan with a **column grid** and dimension strings | Stage 2 SD → 3 DD | Grid bubbles A/B/C · 1/2/3; walls are two lines |
| Plan + section with a resolved **floor-to-floor** | Stage 3 DD | Section shows structure depth + ceiling void |
| RCP with fixture layout | Stage 3 DD | Ceiling grid, luminaires, diffusers |
| Plan with **door tags, window tags, hatching, room data** | Stage 4 CD | Tags in bubbles; poché hatching; a title block |
| 1:5 detail with material call-outs and leader lines | Stage 4 CD | Every layer of the build-up is named |
| Site photo of scaffolding / an RFI question | Stage 5 | It's already being built |
| Photos of a finished building | Stage 6 (or a renovation Stage 0) | Ask which |

**Rule of thumb:** the presence of a **grid** moves them from concept to SD. The presence of **tags and
hatching** moves them from DD to CD. The presence of **dimensions they'd defend** is the line between
"I'm exploring" and "I'm committing".

### When the ask doesn't match the stage

Say so in one line, name what's missing, and offer the right deliverable. Don't refuse; **re-aim**.

- *Concept sketch → "give me a construction detail":*
  "You've sent a concept sketch and asked for a wall detail — the geometry isn't fixed enough to detail
  yet (no floor-to-floor, no façade system, no structure). Here's what I'd resolve first: [grid, core,
  section]. Meanwhile I can give you a **façade parti** and a build-up *strategy* (§64 / §68), which is
  the honest version of a detail at this stage."

- *CD tender set → "make me a dreamy moodboard":*
  "You're at CD — a moodboard now can only cause drift, because the materials are specified. If you need
  a client-facing image, that's a **presentation render off the actual CD geometry** (render type 12,
  §80 consistency), or a **materials board built from the spec** (§46) — not an aspirational one."

- *Dimensioned SD plan → "photoreal 8K hero, night, rain":*
  Fine — but say what it costs: "This will read as final. If the façade is still a direction, not a
  system, I'll render it as an atmosphere and keep the mullion language loose, so nobody signs off a
  curtain wall you haven't priced."

- *No site, no brief → "design me a building":*
  You're at Stage 0. Ask for the plot, the constraints, the area target and the budget band before
  drawing anything. A render without a brief is a picture, not a project.

- *They ask for a render **and** a redesign in the same breath:*
  Split it. The Preservation Contract (SKILL.md §2) means a render never silently redesigns. Offer:
  (a) render as-designed, (b) a **design proposal** (§53) as a clearly separate deliverable, or (c) both,
  labelled.

**Never** let a downstream deliverable smuggle in an upstream decision. A detail implies a section; a
section implies a grid; a grid implies a parti. If you have to invent the upstream to produce the
downstream, you are guessing — say what you'd have to assume, and ask instead.
