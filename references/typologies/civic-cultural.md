# Typology Briefs — Civic & Cultural
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Widely-used rules of thumb for design development. Assembly
> occupancies (theatres, places of worship, schools) carry some of the strictest life-safety rules —
> verify egress and occupant load against local code before any design freeze.
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

---

## Museum / Gallery
**Program ratios** — Typical collecting museum: public gallery **35–50 %** of gross floor area; collection storage **15–25 %** (a real collecting institution stores far more than it shows — 90–95 % of the collection is off view); back-of-house (conservation labs, registrar, workshop, loading, plant) **15–25 %**; public non-gallery (lobby, shop, café, education, WCs, cloaks) **15–20 %**. Gross-to-net factor is high, **1.5–1.7×** — the circulation, plant and envelope for a controlled environment eat the budget. Kunsthalle / non-collecting galleries drop storage to near zero and push gallery to 55–65 %.
**Key metrics** — Gallery clear height **4.0–6.0 m** for a flexible fine-art suite (3.5 m is the practical floor; contemporary art routinely wants 6 m+ and a hatch/lift that can take a crate that size). Visitor density for design: allow **≈ 5 m² per visitor** at comfortable viewing, **2.5–3 m²** at peak. Viewing distance ≈ **1.5–2× the picture height**; hanging centreline commonly **1 450–1 550 mm** AFFL. Illuminance limits (CIE / conservation practice): **≤ 50 lux** for highly sensitive (works on paper, textiles, dyed leather), **≈ 150–200 lux** for oil and tempera, **300 lux** for durable materials (stone, metal, ceramic); UV **≤ 75 µW/lumen**, and manage cumulative annual lux-hours, not just instantaneous level.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Assembly occupancy: egress widths, two remote exits from each gallery suite, travel distance. Conservation environment (commonly cited): **20–24 °C** and **45–55 % RH**, with short-term drift held tight (often ±5 % RH, ±2 °C) — *stability matters more than the setpoint*; loan agreements from major lenders will state their own band and you must meet it. Fire: water-based suppression is contested in collection areas (pre-action / dry-pipe or gas systems in stores and labs); smoke control in tall atria. Accessibility throughout, including to loading and back-of-house.
**Spatial standards** — Freight route sized on the largest anticipated object: **loading bay door 3.0–4.0 m wide × 3.6–4.2 m high** and an unbroken crate route (art lift **≥ 2.5 × 3.0 m car**, no dog-legs, no thresholds) from dock to every gallery. Gallery door leaves **1.8–2.4 m** wide minimum. Circulation ≥ 2.4 m wide where visitors queue at works. Storage: allow **2–3× the gallery area** for a growing collection; mobile/compact racking doubles density but needs **≈ 7.2 kN/m² (150 psf)** structural allowance.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Three unmixed flows: **public** (arrival → free lobby → ticket/threshold → galleries → shop → exit), **staff**, and **collection** (dock → quarantine/receiving → conservation lab → store → gallery, all inside a secured envelope, never crossing the public route). Galleries want a *choice-with-a-spine* plan: a legible primary route, escape valves, and rest points — visitor fatigue sets in around **30–45 minutes**. Lobby carries orientation, security screening, cloaks and the group/school split before the paying threshold.
**Common mistakes** — Sunlight straight onto artwork (a glazed south wall behind a canvas is a conservation failure, not a mood); glazing without louvres, UV film and a lux budget. Sizing storage from today's collection, not 25 years of accessions. A crate route with a step, a tight lift or a 2.1 m door — the show physically cannot get in. Treating HVAC as a fit-out afterthought: the plant for a Class-A environment is **often 10–15 % of gross area** and needs its own vertical zones. Continuous enfilade with no seats and no daylight relief. Skylights that dump direct beam instead of north-washed/diffused light.
**Render notes** — **Lighting is the whole game.** Convincing museum renders read as *controlled* light: soft ambient wash on walls, a discrete accent gradient on each work (track/framing projector, not a uniform flood), a dark-adapted floor, and warm-cool contrast between gallery and daylit lobby. Show the light *fittings* — track, slot, snoot — they are the visible grammar of the type. Model the works as low-contrast, low-saturation placeholders unless licensed; put a person 1.5–2 m from a canvas at the correct sightline. Fake tells: artwork lit as brightly as the room, no visible luminaires, empty white voids with no seating/vitrines/labels, glossy mirror-polished floor bouncing gallery lights, and daylight blazing through onto the art.

## School (primary / secondary)
**Program ratios** — Teaching space **45–55 %** of gross; circulation **20–25 %** (never below ~18 % in a compliant school); hall / dining / sports **10–15 %**; staff and admin **5–8 %**; WCs, plant, stores the balance. Gross-to-net **≈ 1.5×**. Non-net area is where schools go over budget — a corridor-heavy double-loaded bar is efficient on paper and miserable in use.
**Key metrics** — Widely-published rules of thumb: **primary ≈ 2.0–2.5 m² net teaching area per pupil**; **secondary ≈ 2.2–3.0 m²** per pupil in general teaching, more in practical/lab/art spaces (**3.0–4.5 m²**). A **30-pupil general classroom lands at ≈ 55–70 m²**. Total gross per pupil, all-in: **≈ 7–10 m²** primary, **≈ 9–12 m²** secondary (varies widely by jurisdiction — treat as a sanity check, not a target). Ceiling **2.7–3.0 m** in classrooms; **3.0–3.5 m+** in labs and halls.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load for classrooms is dense (order of **1.8–2.0 m² net per person** in common codes) → two exits and generous stair capacity. Acoustics is a hard driver, not a nicety: indoor ambient noise commonly limited to **≈ 35 dB L<sub>Aeq</sub>** in a teaching space and reverberation to **≈ 0.6–0.8 s** — this dictates ceiling absorption and glazing/ventilation strategy. Daylight and ventilation: target daylight factor **≈ 2–5 %** average with glare control; fresh air / CO₂ limits drive window area and openable %. Accessibility, safeguarding (controlled single entrance, sightlines to play areas), fire compartmentation.
**Spatial standards** — Classroom proportion is the lever: keep **depth ≤ ~2.5× the window head height** for unilateral daylight (a 3.0 m head ⇒ ~7.5 m deep). A square-ish 8 × 8 m room with one window wall is already daylight-starved at the back. Corridors: **1.8 m** absolute minimum, **2.4–3.0 m** where lockers, coats or queuing occur; widen at classroom doors. Doors out of teaching spaces open in the direction of escape where the occupant load requires it. Stair widths sized on the surge, not the average.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Cluster by age/department around a shared social heart (year-group commons, resource pod) rather than one endless corridor. Noisy (hall, sports, music, D&T) separated acoustically and by buffer from quiet (classrooms, library). Single supervised public entry → reception with visual control of the door; deliveries and kitchen on their own route. Direct external access from early-years/primary classrooms to outdoor teaching. Toilets on the main circulation, visible but not overlooked — a supervision problem, not a plumbing one.
**Common mistakes** — Deep plan classrooms that kill daylight and force lights on all day. Hard-surfaced, glazed "collaboration atria" with a 3-second reverberation time — visually great, acoustically unteachable. Under-sizing circulation and storage (every school runs out of store space in year one). Ignoring the **10-minute peak**: corridors and stairs must clear a whole cohort at changeover. Glare from unshaded south/west glazing onto whiteboards and screens. Forgetting a coat/bag strategy — bags on the floor of a 1.8 m corridor is a fire route problem.
**Render notes** — Schools read as fake when they are empty and pristine. Populate: real children at the right **height** for the age group (a primary classroom with adult-scale figures is instantly wrong), furniture at 1:1 (chair seat 260–430 mm by age), pinboards with work on them, bags, mess. Daylight-dominant lighting, artificial as fill; show the shading device (brise-soleil, blind, deep reveal) actually doing something. Fake tells: no acoustic ceiling, no visible services in a hall, immaculate white walls, and a "collaboration stair" nobody could actually supervise.

## University Building (teaching / faculty)
**Program ratios** — Assignable (net) space typically **55–65 %** of gross — teaching (lecture, seminar, studio, lab), academic offices, research, and student social. Circulation **20–25 %**, plant **5–15 %** (much higher for wet-lab or engineering buildings, where services can drive floor-to-floor to **4.2–4.5 m**). A humanities building and a science building with the same GFA are not the same building — resolve lab/plant demand before massing.
**Key metrics** — Indicative net areas per station: **lecture theatre (tiered, fixed seats) ≈ 0.6–0.9 m² per seat**; **flat seminar / flexible classroom ≈ 1.8–2.5 m² per student**; **studio ≈ 3.5–5.0 m² per student** (architecture/art, with a permanent desk); **wet lab ≈ 4.5–6.0 m² per student position**. Academic office **≈ 10–14 m²**; shared/postgrad workstation **≈ 4.5–6.0 m²**. Space is only justified by **utilisation** — a lecture theatre at 30 % occupancy is the most expensive mistake on the estate.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Assembly rules govern the lecture theatres (occupant load = number of fixed seats; two remote exits; aisle and cross-aisle widths). Accessibility: wheelchair positions **dispersed across the rake**, with companion seats and an accessible route to the lectern/stage — not one row at the back. Labs: fume-hood face velocity and make-up air, containment levels, chemical storage, emergency egress from a lab is unforgiving. Acoustics and AV (speech transmission index) in every teaching room.
**Spatial standards** — Tiered lecture theatre: row-to-row (back-to-back) **850–1 000 mm**; seat width **500–600 mm**; writing tablet or continuous bench desk adds depth. **Sightline C-value 60–125 mm** (60 mm = seeing *between* the heads in front, staggered seating; 120–125 mm = over the heads, a steeper and more expensive rake). Maximum seats between aisles per code (commonly **14**, i.e. 7 from an aisle, unless continental spacing is used). Rear-row viewing distance to screen ≤ **6× screen height** for legible text. Ceiling at the rear of a big rake climbs fast — check the section early.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Teaching (high-traffic, timetabled, ground/lower floors) → research and offices (quiet, upper floors) is the default stratification; big lecture theatres want direct access off the main lobby so a 300-person changeover never crosses the research floors. Lecture theatre needs a **generous foyer/breakout to absorb the emptying cohort** — the corridor outside is not enough. Labs need a service/goods route (gas cylinders, waste) independent of the student route.
**Common mistakes** — Designing lecture theatres with a flat floor "for flexibility" and getting neither good sightlines nor good group work. Accessible seating dumped at one end of the back row. Ignoring changeover surge: 300 out and 300 in, in 10 minutes, through one door. Under-provisioning informal study seats — the modern university is used for the space *between* classes. Locking floor-to-floor height before the lab services are known, then discovering there is no ceiling void.
**Render notes** — Show the **rake and the section** — an exterior or lobby render of a university building that hides the theatre's underside is dodging the interesting move. Populate at plausible density (a lecture theatre at 100 % looks staged; 60–75 % reads true). Correct AV: a lit screen, a lectern, a visible lighting scene where the front is bright and the seats are dim. Fake tells: students all facing forward, motionless, evenly spaced; no bags, laptops or coats; a lecture theatre with no visible acoustic treatment or lighting on the rear wall.

## Library
**Program ratios** — Contemporary public/academic library: **reader and public space 40–55 %** (and rising), **collection/stacks 20–35 %** (and falling), staff and technical services **10–15 %**, circulation/plant/WC the rest. The programme has inverted in a generation: the building is now mostly *people space with books in it*, not a book warehouse with a reading room.
**Key metrics** — Indicative capacities: a standard double-sided open stack range holds **≈ 150–200 volumes per m²** of the floor area it occupies (aisle share included); ~**30 volumes per linear metre** of shelf, **6–7 shelves** per 2.1–2.3 m bay. **Compact/mobile shelving roughly doubles** that density. Reader stations: **2.3–2.8 m² per reader** in an open reading room; **3.5–4.5 m²** for a carrel or a study bay including circulation. Typical carrel top **900 × 600 mm**; a shared table gives **≥ 900 × 700 mm per reader**.
**Code & compliance drivers** — **Structural loading is the defining constraint**: book stacks are commonly designed at **≈ 7.2 kN/m² (150 psf)**, reading rooms at **≈ 2.9 kN/m² (60 psf)**. Compact shelving pushes higher still. If you want to move the stacks later, the *whole floor plate* must be designed at stack loading — this is the single most consequential early decision. Assembly egress in reading rooms; accessibility of aisles; conservation environment for special collections and archives (same 45–55 % RH stability logic as museums, tighter for film/photo).
**Spatial standards** — Stack aisle **900 mm minimum**, **1 000–1 100 mm** for wheelchair use, **1 200 mm+** in high-traffic ranges; range centre-to-centre (double-sided, 250–300 mm shelves) **≈ 1 400–1 600 mm**. Shelving height: **1 800 mm** for open public access (top shelf within reach), up to 2 100–2 300 mm in staff/closed stacks. Aisle *length* limited by egress — long dead-end ranges are an escape problem. Ceiling **2.7 m+** over stacks, higher over reading rooms to get daylight deep.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Arrival → welcome/self-issue → a graded **zoning by noise**: loud (café, children's, group study, events) near the entrance, quiet study deepest and highest, silent/special collections most controlled. Staff workroom adjacent to returns/sorting and the goods route. Special collections/archive: a supervised reading room with lockers outside, a single controlled door, and no public access to the store. Daylight and view to the *readers*; books to the interior/shaded side.
**Common mistakes** — Direct sun on the stacks (paper bleaches and cockles) and on reading surfaces (glare, then everyone closes the blind and you daylight nothing). Designing a beautiful central atrium and then discovering the whole building is one acoustic volume — a library needs *graded* quiet, which an open void destroys. Sizing the floor plate for readers and then being asked to add stacks it cannot carry. Aisles at 900 mm with a trolley and a wheelchair in them. Forgetting power/data density at every reader seat.
**Render notes** — Books are the texture — **model real shelf variation** (heights, spine colours, gaps, a leaning run, a trolley). A stack render with identical repeated books is the tell. Light: soft ambient from above, task light at the desk, daylight raking from a side/clerestory, no glare on screens. People should be doing library things (reading, laptop, browsing, a librarian at the desk) at believable density — a half-full reading room, not a stadium. Fake tells: mirror floors, uniformly bright stacks, no signage or end-panels, no cables/sockets, and no acoustic ceiling anywhere.

## Religious Building
> Wildly variable by faith, denomination and rite — treat the numbers below as generic assembly-space
> geometry and confirm liturgical requirements with the client/community first. Orientation, gender
> arrangement, ablution, ritual axis and seating vs. standing vs. prostration are **not** design variables
> you choose.

**Program ratios** — Main worship hall **50–65 %** of gross; ancillary community/education (halls, classrooms, kitchen, meeting) **20–30 %**; ablution/WC, entry, storage, staff/clergy the rest. Many contemporary projects are really *community centres with a worship hall* — the ancillary programme is used far more hours per week than the hall, and should not be treated as leftover space.
**Key metrics** — Seated on pews/benches: **≈ 450–500 mm of bench length per person**; row (back-to-back) pitch **800–900 mm**. Loose-chair seating: **≈ 0.6–0.9 m² per seated worshipper** including row circulation. Standing/prostration (mosque prayer-row module): **≈ 1.2 m deep × 0.7–0.8 m wide per person**, i.e. **≈ 0.85–1.0 m² per worshipper**, plus aisles — the hall area is driven by a whole number of full **rows parallel to the qibla**, so the *proportion* of the hall matters as much as its area. Occupant load for egress on unfixed/standing assembly is dense (commonly **≈ 0.65 m²/person** concentrated) — the exits, not the ritual, may set the wall.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Assembly occupancy at its most demanding, because the building is designed for the **peak festival crowd**, not the weekly attendance: size egress on the peak, or accept an explicit, documented cap. Exits, aisle widths, panic hardware, travel distance. Accessibility to the ritual centre (altar/bimah/minbar/sanctuary), not just to the seats. Acoustics: amplified speech vs. unamplified chant/organ pull in opposite directions. Fire safety around candles, incense, timber galleries.
**Spatial standards** — Main aisle **1 200–1 800 mm** (processional aisles wider); side aisles per egress calc, typically **≥ 1 100 mm**. Sightlines to the ritual focus from every seat — a flat floor works only to a limited depth (**~15–20 m** before rear seats lose the celebrant); beyond that, rake the floor or raise the platform. Ceiling height in the hall **6–12 m+** is common and is doing acoustic and spiritual work simultaneously. Ablution facilities sized on the peak and placed *on the route* from entrance to hall, with drainage and a wet/dry transition.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Arrival → threshold/narthex/courtyard (a decompression space; also where the community actually meets) → hall, on the ritual axis. Clergy/officiant route (vestry, mihrab-side, back-of-platform) separate from the congregation. Where the rite requires separate or screened areas, resolve them as *equal-quality* spaces with their own dignified entry and full sightlines, not as an afterthought balcony. Community wing accessible when the hall is closed — a separate lockable entrance is worth its cost.
**Common mistakes** — Designing for the average congregation and failing on the festival day. Sizing a mosque hall by area alone and getting a proportion that wastes half a prayer row. A cathedral-height, hard-surfaced volume with amplified speech and a **3–5 s reverberation time** — gloriously reverberant, completely unintelligible; either the acoustic serves the *dominant* rite or you need variable absorption and a distributed/delay speech system. Forgetting the accessible route to the platform. Under-sizing ablution/WC and storage (chairs, instruments, seasonal items).
**Render notes** — This type lives or dies on **light as material**: shafts, washes, a slot, a lantern, a screened/perforated wall throwing patterned light. Get the *direction and time* right — a raking low sun through a clerestory reads as intentional; flat uniform ambient reads as an empty hall. Show the ritual focus lit and the congregation zone dimmer. Fake tells: an unpopulated hall (use worshippers at correct posture — seated, standing, or in prostration rows); the carpet/prayer-mat module misaligned with the qibla; incorrect or invented religious iconography; and glossy floors bouncing every downlight.

## Cultural Centre / Theatre
**Program ratios** — For a producing theatre, the auditorium is a **minority** of the building. Indicative: **house (audience chamber) 20–25 %** of gross; **stage + wings + fly tower 10–20 %**; **front-of-house** (foyer, bars, box office, cloaks, WCs) **15–25 %**; **back-of-house** (dressing rooms, green room, wardrobe, workshops, scene dock, get-in, offices, plant) **30–40 %**. Any scheme where BOH looks small is a scheme that has not been designed yet.
**Key metrics** — House area **≈ 0.6–0.8 m² per seat** (chamber to comfortable), plus FOH foyer **≈ 0.3–0.5 m² per person** at the interval crush. Acoustic volume: **≈ 4–6 m³/seat** for drama, **6–8 m³/seat** for opera/musical, **8–10 m³/seat** for a symphonic concert hall. Occupied reverberation time: **drama 0.9–1.1 s**, **opera 1.4–1.6 s**, **symphonic 1.8–2.2 s** — these are *different buildings*, and "multi-purpose" means variable acoustics (drapes, banners, reflectors), not a compromise number. Orchestra pit **≈ 1.0–1.5 m² per musician**.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Assembly egress at maximum severity: occupant load = fixed seats + standing; exits remote, discharging to the street without re-entering the building; **aisles ≥ 1 100 mm** (and widening toward the exit as they accumulate); cross-aisles and vomitories sized on the flow. Seats per row between aisles: conventionally a maximum of **14** (7 from any aisle); **continental seating** allows far more (20–25+) but only with increased row spacing (**≥ 1 000–1 100 mm** back-to-back) and side exits at every row. Fire: proscenium safety curtain, stage smoke vent, separation of stage from house. Dispersed wheelchair spaces with companion seats and an accessible route to the stage.
**Spatial standards** — Seat width **500–560 mm** (450 mm absolute minimum, armless); row-to-row (back-to-back) **850–950 mm** conventional. **Sightline C-value 60 mm** (staggered seating, seeing between heads) to **120–125 mm** (over heads) — this single number sets the rake and therefore the section, the balcony and the whole building height. Proscenium opening **9–14 m** typical for a mid-scale house; **stage depth ≥ proscenium width**; **wing space ≥ ½ proscenium width each side**; **fly tower clear height ≈ 2.5× the proscenium opening height** (so a 7 m opening ⇒ ~17–18 m grid). Get-in: door **≥ 2.4–3.0 m wide × 3.6–4.2 m high**, level (or dock-levelled) route from an articulated-lorry standing position to the stage with **no** steps, no tight corners, no low soffits.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Three independent worlds: **audience** (street → foyer → box office/bar → house), **performer** (stage door → dressing rooms → green room → wings, never through the foyer), **scenery** (lorry → get-in → scene dock → stage, at stage level). Foyer must absorb the whole house in the interval **and** hold it before the show — it is the building's public face and its social product. WCs sized on a 20-minute interval, not a working day (and heavily weighted to female provision). Dressing rooms want daylight and a route to the stage that does not cross a public corridor.
**Common mistakes** — Ignoring the **get-in**: designing a beautiful theatre a set cannot physically enter is the classic non-specialist failure. Under-sizing the fly tower or wings, so nothing can be flown or struck. A foyer sized for arrival but not for the interval crush. Treating "multi-purpose hall" as free — without variable acoustics and a flexible seating/stage system it is a bad theatre *and* a bad concert hall. Wheelchair spaces only at the back or only at the front. Forgetting the scene dock, the wardrobe, the laundry, the bin store — the unglamorous 30 % that makes it work.
**Render notes** — **Lighting is the subject.** A theatre interior render needs a real lighting state: stage warm and bright, house dim with low-level aisle/step lighting and seat-back glow, a visible lighting rig / FOH bridge / follow-spot position. Show the *equipment* — bars, lanterns, cabling, the grid — its absence is the fastest tell. Foyer renders want the **night, lit-from-within** condition, spilling to the street, with a crowd, glasses, coats: the theatre's exterior is only ever seen at dusk or in the dark. Fake tells: an evenly lit empty auditorium with no rig; seats rendered as an untextured repeated block; a stage with no depth, no wings and no fly-lines visible; and a foyer with three people in it.
