# Typology Briefs — Residential
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Widely-used rules of thumb for design development. Residential
> minimum room sizes, stair counts and egress rules vary enormously by jurisdiction — verify locally.
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

---

## Single-Family House / Luxury Villa

**Program ratios** — Circulation typically ~10–15% of net floor area in a well-planned house; above ~20% the plan is corridor-heavy. Net-to-gross is rarely tracked as such (there is no core), but wall thickness + circulation usually accounts for ~15–20% of the footprint. Garage: allow ~15–20 m² per car (single bay commonly ~3.0 × 6.0 m / 10 × 20 ft). Terrace/outdoor living in villas is often programmed at 20–40% of indoor area; guest/staff wings in luxury work commonly add 15–25%.

**Key metrics** — Typical spans: 4–6 m for timber joists, 5–8 m for one-way concrete slabs; beyond ~7 m expect downstands or post-tension, which changes the ceiling reading. Floor-to-floor 3.0–3.6 m (10–12 ft) in mid-range, 3.6–4.5 m in luxury. Indicative area bands per bedroom type: single/child 9–12 m², double 12–16 m², principal suite with bath + closet 25–45 m². Plan depth for a naturally lit room: rule of thumb is habitable depth ≈ 2–2.5× the head height of the window; single-sided rooms deeper than ~6–7 m go dark at the back.

**Code & compliance drivers** — Egress: habitable rooms and basements typically require a second means of escape (an escape window/door with minimum clear opening and a maximum sill height, or a protected stair). Protected escape stair/hall in multi-storey houses; interlinked smoke alarms on every level. Guardrails on any level change above ~0.6–1.0 m, with a maximum sphere-passable gap (commonly 100 mm / 4 in). Fire separation to attached garages and between semi-detached/terraced units. Accessible/visitable ground floor (level entry, WC) is mandatory in many jurisdictions. Daylight/ventilation minimums are usually expressed as a glazed area ≥ ~10% of floor area and openable area ≥ ~5%.

**Spatial standards** — Corridors ≥ 900 mm (1000–1200 mm is comfortable); doors 800–900 mm clear leaf, 850–900 mm where accessibility applies. Stairs: ≥ 900 mm wide (1000–1100 mm preferred), rise ~170–190 mm, going ~250–280 mm, headroom ≥ 2.0–2.1 m. Kitchen: 1000–1200 mm clear between opposing runs (1200–1500 mm for two cooks); counter depth 600 mm, height ~900 mm. Bathroom: ~700–750 mm clear in front of a WC, ~750–900 mm in front of a basin. Ceiling height ≥ 2.4 m (2.7–3.0 m reads as generous; 3.5 m+ is villa territory).

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Entry → living/dining/kitchen (public) → family/study (semi-private) → bedrooms (private), with the gradient expressed in floors, wings, or a spine. Served (living, sleeping) vs servant (utility, plant, storage, pantry, laundry, garage) — servants band along the coldest/least-viewed edge, freeing the good orientation and the view for served space. Kitchen wants a direct service line to the garage/entry (groceries) and to outdoor dining. Guest WC near the entry, never off the living room. Principal suite gets the best orientation and the deepest privacy buffer.

**Common mistakes** — Deep single-aspect plans with dark middles (no clerestory, no light well, no dual aspect). Over-glazed west facades in hot climates: late-afternoon low sun is the hardest to shade — brise-soleil are useless on a west face; use vertical fins, deep reveals, or landscape. Double-height voids sized for the render, not for the acoustics or the heating bill. Stairs landing in the middle of the living room. Cantilevered slabs and balcony edges continued straight through the envelope as uninsulated concrete = thermal bridges and condensation. Programming a "media room" and a "family room" and a "living room" in a house that only needs one. Roof overhangs drawn thin because they look elegant, then failing to shade anything.

**Render notes** — Convincing: lived-in cues (a chair pulled out, a coat, a glass on the counter, a rug that doesn't align to the grid), furniture at real scale (a sofa is ~850–900 mm deep, a dining chair seat ~450 mm high — undersized furniture is the #1 tell of a fake render), warm interior light spilling out at dusk, one or two imperfections. Catalogue tells: everything symmetric and centred, a floating bowl of green apples, zero cables, no light switches, over-uniform grass, an empty pool with no water line, and every surface at the same specular level.

## Apartment Building & Residential Tower

**Program ratios** — Net-to-gross (saleable/net residential ÷ GFA) is the number the project lives or dies on: ~80–85% is efficient for a mid-rise slab, ~75–82% for a typical tower, and dropping under ~70% usually means the core or the corridor is eating the building. Core (stairs + lifts + lift lobby + risers + refuse) commonly ~10–20% of the floorplate — the higher the tower, the bigger the core (more lifts, pressurised stairs, more risers). Horizontal circulation ~8–15%. Balconies are typically excluded from GFA in many jurisdictions (check — this drives whether they are "free" area or not). Parking commonly 0.5–1.5 spaces/unit depending on transit; allow ~25–30 m² per space including aisles.

**Key metrics** — Units per core per floor: 4–8 is the comfortable band for a double-loaded corridor; 8–12+ starts producing dark, deep units; single-stair point blocks (where permitted) typically cap at ~4–6 units/floor. Floor-to-floor 2.9–3.3 m is the standard economic range (structure + services + ~2.4–2.6 m clear). Structural grid usually aligned to the parking bay below (~8.1–8.4 m column spacing is the classic parking-driven grid). Unit depths: 8–12 m from facade to corridor is typical; beyond ~12 m the back of the unit is unusable without borrowed light. Indicative unit areas (highly market-dependent): studio 25–40 m², 1-bed 45–60 m², 2-bed 65–90 m², 3-bed 90–120 m².

**Code & compliance drivers** — **Egress is the single biggest plan driver.** Most codes require two independent exit stairs above a threshold defined by height, travel distance, and units per floor; a growing number of jurisdictions permit a *single-stair* point block below a height limit (varies widely, commonly in the 5–7 storey range in the codes that allow it) subject to sprinklers, a pressurised/smoke-protected stair, limited units per floor, and a short travel distance. Never assume single-stair is available — it is the most jurisdiction-specific rule in this typology. Also: maximum travel distance to an exit and dead-end corridor limits; fire-rated compartmentation between dwellings (unit = its own fire compartment, typically 60–120 min); protected corridors and lobbies; smoke control/ventilation of the corridor and stair; firefighting lift + firefighting shaft above a height threshold; a defined proportion of accessible/adaptable units; acoustic separation between dwellings (party walls and floors have a tested minimum airborne and impact rating — this is a *legal* performance requirement, not a nicety).

**Spatial standards** — Corridors typically ≥ 1200 mm clear (1500 mm where wheelchair passing or turning is required). Egress stairs commonly ≥ 1100–1200 mm clear width, with landings at least the stair width. Apartment entrance doors 850–900 mm clear. Ceiling clear height ≥ 2.4 m (2.5–2.7 m sells). Lift lobby ≥ 1800 mm deep in front of the doors. Balcony minimum useful depth ~1.8–2.0 m (below ~1.5 m nobody puts a table on it — it becomes a drying rack). Kitchen and bathroom clearances as per the house above.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — The core drives everything: it is the only fixed element, it must stack, and it must land in the parking/podium grid below. Public (street, lobby, amenity) → semi-private (corridor, lift lobby, shared terrace) → private (unit door) → intimate (bedrooms). Within the unit, the entry hall is the servant zone (WC, storage, mechanical), the living/kitchen takes the facade and the view, and bedrooms take the quiet side. Risers, refuse chute and services stack in the core — every shift in the core between floors costs money. Bedrooms of one unit should never share a party wall with the living room or the stair of the next; stack wet rooms above wet rooms.

**Common mistakes** — Cores that waste GFA: an oversized, poorly-shaped core placed in the best-lit part of the plan instead of the dark middle. Deep double-loaded plans with dark middles and single-aspect units that cannot cross-ventilate. Corridors with no daylight at either end (a window at the end of a corridor is worth more than it costs). Balconies cast as continuous slab extensions = a thermal bridge round the entire floorplate; use structural thermal breaks or separate the balcony structure. Over-glazed west facades in hot climates on a repeated floorplate — the mistake gets multiplied by 30 storeys. Designing the tower plan then trying to land the core in the parking grid afterwards. Chasing a "signature" facade that produces 40 different unit types (the developer will value-engineer it out).

**Render notes** — Convincing: differentiated life on every balcony (a bike, a plant that is dying, laundry, a barbecue, mismatched chairs), some blinds down and some up, a few lights on and a few off at dusk, subtly different curtain colours behind the glass. Reflections that show a *real* sky and a *real* neighbouring building, not a generic HDRI. Catalogue tells: every balcony identically furnished with the same two grey chairs, every window with the same reflection, no window treatments at all, a perfectly uniform facade with no ageing or joint lines, a ground floor with pristine glass and no signage, and people all walking in the same direction.

## Penthouse

**Program ratios** — Circulation is generous by definition (5–15%), but the deal is made in the outdoor ratio: private terrace often 30–60%+ of the interior area, and a plunge pool, spa, or pergola in the top tier. Expect back-of-house to grow: staff/service entry, service kitchen, plant, storage — commonly 10–20% of the unit and always on the blind, un-viewed side. Private lift lobby (or a lift opening directly into the unit) is standard at this level.

**Key metrics** — Floor-to-floor is usually raised above the typical floor — 3.3–4.5 m or more — because the setback and the roof plant give the room. Column-free living is the aspiration: spans of 9–12 m+ (transfer structure or a stiffer top-floor frame). Terrace parapets ≥ 1.1 m (typical guardrail height) — but a 1.1 m solid parapet cuts the seated view, so glass balustrade or a recessed parapet is the standard move. Two exits still apply: a penthouse is not exempt from egress.

**Code & compliance drivers** — Same egress rules as the tower below (two protected exits from the floor unless the jurisdiction's single-stair rules apply); often a firefighting lift and, on very tall towers, a refuge floor within a defined vertical distance. Roof access and safe-maintenance access to the facade and terrace. Wind: at the top of a tower, terraces need wind studies — a beautiful roof terrace that is unusable 200 days a year is a design failure, not a weather problem. Guardrails, glass balustrade impact class, and a maximum climbable-detail / gap standard. Waterproofing and drainage of an occupied roof/terrace above habitable space is the highest-risk detail in the building.

**Spatial standards** — Clear ceiling 2.9–3.6 m+. Doors to principal spaces often 1000 mm+ leaves (a 900 mm door in a penthouse reads cheap). Circulation ≥ 1200 mm. Internal stair (duplex penthouse) ≥ 1000–1200 mm and treated as a feature, but it still needs the same rise/going and headroom (≥ 2.0–2.1 m) as any other stair. Terrace usable depth ≥ 3 m for dining, ≥ 4–5 m for a lounge + circulation.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Arrival is the entire point: private lobby → a compressed threshold → release into the view. The view side is served space (living, dining, principal suite); the core side and the plant/setback side is servant (kitchen back-of-house, staff, storage, laundry, plant). Guest quarters buffer from the principal suite. The terrace should be entered from at least two rooms, not one — a terrace with a single door is a balcony with ambitions.

**Common mistakes** — A penthouse that is just the typical floorplate with the walls removed — same core, same corridor, same low ceiling, no extra height, no privacy. Over-glazed west facades with no shading, because "the sunset view" — then the client installs blackout blinds and the render is a lie. Terraces sized for the plan, not for the wind. Plant equipment parked on the terrace and drawn out of the render. Ignoring the roof: a penthouse looks up as much as out, so a flat plasterboard ceiling at 2.6 m wastes the one thing a top floor has. Uplifting the finishes without uplifting the structure — visible downstand beams every 6 m in a "column-free" living room.

**Render notes** — Convincing: the view is the hero and must be a *real*, coherent city at the right height and haze — the horizon line has to sit where the floor level implies it. Dusk with warm interior + cool sky, city lights below at plausible density, a reflection of the interior in the glass (never a perfectly transparent full-height window at night). One glass of wine, not a styled bar. Catalogue tells: a horizon that is too close, a pin-sharp city with no atmospheric perspective, glass with zero reflection, an immaculate terrace with no wind and no shadow of the parapet, and a grand piano nobody plays.

## Tiny Home / Modular-Prefab

**Program ratios** — Circulation approaches zero by design: in a compact plan, dedicated circulation should be under ~5–10% — every corridor is a failure. Storage is the real ratio: 10–15% of floor area in built-in storage is a working target, and under-bed/under-stair/loft volume counts. Net-to-gross is dominated by wall thickness (a 200–300 mm insulated wall costs a *lot* of a 20 m² footprint). Service core (bath + kitchen + utilities on one wet wall) should be a single compact block.

**Key metrics** — Typical tiny home: 15–40 m² (160–400 ft²). Road-transportable modules are governed by transport limits, not by design: in most places an untrimmed load is roughly ≤ 2.5–3.0 m wide before permits and escorts, so a module tends to land around 3–4.5 m wide × 9–18 m long — **verify the actual limits and permit thresholds for the road route, they vary by country and by state/province.** Height on a trailer is limited by the road envelope (commonly around 4.0–4.3 m total including the trailer, again jurisdiction-specific). Loft sleeping spaces are typically 1.0–1.4 m clear (below full standing height — legal as sleeping space in some jurisdictions, not in others). Stacked/volumetric modular buildings: module depth usually 3.0–4.2 m internal, spans short, with the module walls doing the structural work.

**Code & compliance drivers** — The critical fork: is it a *building* (permanent foundation → full building code, full egress, full ceiling heights, energy code, accessibility) or a *vehicle/caravan/RV* (road/RV standards, far looser, often not legal as a permanent dwelling)? These two paths have almost nothing in common — establish which one applies before designing. For buildings: egress window from every sleeping space, ceiling height minimums (which is exactly what a sleeping loft violates), stair vs ladder rules for the loft, smoke/CO alarms, and — for stacked modular — inter-module fire compartmentation and acoustic separation, which are *harder* in modular because of the cavity between modules. Volumetric modular also carries factory inspection / third-party certification regimes in many jurisdictions.

**Spatial standards** — The compact set: kitchen aisle 900–1000 mm absolute minimum (1000–1200 mm preferred); WC clear zone ~700–750 mm in front; shower ≥ 800 × 800 mm (900 × 900 mm to be humane); main clear height ≥ 2.1–2.4 m; a fixed stair to a loft still wants ≥ 600–700 mm width and, if it is a ladder, it needs a code exception. Counter height 900 mm, depth 600 mm — do not "compact" these two, the body doesn't shrink. Doors 700–800 mm internally, 800–900 mm at entry.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — One wet wall, everything else free. The plan is a gradient along the long axis: entry/utility → kitchen (servant) → living (public) → sleeping (private, or up in the loft). Furniture is the architecture: a stair that is storage, a bench that is a bed, a table that folds. Adjacency rule of the typology: never put circulation *through* the living space to reach the bed — put the bed at the far end and let the living space *be* the circulation. Prefab adds a second logic layer: the module joint line must be respected — plan across it, don't fight it, and put the wet core wholly inside one module so the plumbing doesn't cross a site joint.

**Common mistakes** — Designing a tiny house as a shrunken normal house (a hallway, a swing door that eats 0.8 m², a room you can't stand in). Ignoring the transport envelope until the end. Loft with no egress and no standing height, sold as a bedroom. Over-glazing a tiny volume: a small box with a big south/west window overheats within an hour — this typology has almost no thermal mass to absorb it. Under-insulating because the wall would get too thick, then discovering the whole thing is a fridge in winter and an oven in summer. Modular: expressing the module joint as a "feature" and getting a facade that reads as a stack of shipping containers when nobody wanted that. Forgetting where the plant goes (a water heater and an MVHU still have real dimensions).

**Render notes** — Convincing: the human figure is essential — the whole point is legibility of scale, and a person standing at the counter or ducking into the loft sells it instantly. Show it *inhabited and cramped-but-happy*: boots at the door, a kettle, an open book, real storage full of real things. Site it honestly (a trailer has wheels; a module has a crane joint and a foundation). Catalogue tells: a tiny home rendered with an empty spotless interior (the entire premise is that the space is worked hard — an empty tiny home is a coffin), a loft with implausible headroom, furniture scaled 80% to make the room look bigger, and a forest backdrop with no access road, no services, and no dirt.
