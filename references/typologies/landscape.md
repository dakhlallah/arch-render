# Typology Briefs — Landscape Architecture
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, and entirely climate-dependent.** Planting advice is meaningless without a
> climate zone and a maintenance regime — always establish both before recommending species. Structural
> loading of roof gardens and any water feature must be signed off by an engineer.
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

**Scope note.** This file treats landscape as a *design discipline* — ground, planting, water, light, and the
detail of the outdoor room. It deliberately does not repeat masterplanning, block structure, or public-realm
network strategy: see `ref-urban.md` §29 / §35 / §51 for those. Units are metric; imperial only where the
convention is genuinely imperial. Where a figure varies by jurisdiction it is given as an indicative range and
labelled as such — verify against the local code, the soil report, and the client's maintenance budget.

## Private garden & courtyard

**What drives the design** — The maintenance regime, first and always: a garden is a design that must be
*kept*, and the honest question is how many hours a month someone will actually give it. Then microclimate —
a courtyard is a thermal and wind trap, and its orientation and enclosure ratio determine sun hours, frost
pockets, and whether it is a refuge or an oven. Then soil: depth, drainage, pH, and whether it is real soil or
builder's rubble with 100 mm of topsoil dressed over it. Privacy and sightlines from adjacent windows drive
screening. Access for machinery and material delivery drives what can ever be changed later.

**Key metrics** — Enclosure: courtyards with a height-to-width ratio above roughly 1:1 lose most direct
midday sun in temperate latitudes and read as cool, shaded, and often damp; below ~1:2 they behave like open
ground. Topsoil for lawn: typically 150–200 mm minimum; for shrub beds 400–600 mm; for small trees the root
zone is measured in cubic metres, not depth (see Streetscape). Main circulation paths 1.2 m clear (two people
abreast); secondary/service paths 0.9 m; a wheelchair-usable route needs 1.2 m clear with 1.5 × 1.5 m passing
and turning spaces at intervals. Terrace/paving falls 1–2% away from the building; never less than 1% on any
paved surface. Step risers 100–150 mm outdoors (shallower than indoors) with generous goings; outdoor stairs
with a single step are a trip hazard — use three or none. Raised beds 400–600 mm high double as seating.

**Technical drivers** — Drainage before everything: falls, a defined outfall, and a sub-base that does not
pond. Level thresholds at the house need a slot drain and a drainage layer, not optimism. Where the courtyard
sits over a basement or garage it is a podium, not a garden — waterproofing, root barrier, drainage board, and
a structural load allowance (saturated substrate is heavy; see Roof garden). Irrigation: assume it will be
needed for at least the establishment period (typically 1–3 growing seasons) even in a "dry" planting scheme,
and design in a tap and a route for it now. Retaining walls over ~600 mm are structures. Lighting and power
conduits go in *before* the paving.

**Planting logic** — Right plant, right place. Match species to climate zone, aspect (a north-facing courtyard
wall in the northern hemisphere is a shade garden, whatever the client wants), water availability, and soil.
Design for the plant's **mature size**, not its nursery size — the single most common failure in private
gardens is a hedge or specimen tree planted at 1.5 m that reaches 8 m and eats the garden. Build a structure of
evergreen bones and long-season foliage first, then layer seasonal interest; a garden that is only good for
three weeks in May is a failure. Prefer fewer species in larger drifts over a plant collection. In hot-dry
climates, cluster the thirsty plants near the terrace and let the rest of the ground go drought-tolerant.

**Common mistakes** — Planting the mature-size tree the client wants *today* in a plot that cannot hold it in
20 years. Lawns in deep shade or in climates that cannot support them without constant irrigation. Level
paving with no fall, then a permanent damp line on the wall. Courtyards paved wall-to-wall with no soil
volume, then "planters" too shallow to keep anything alive through a heatwave. Specifying a planting palette
without ever asking who waters it. Retaining the topsoil in a heap that gets driven over and compacted to
death during construction.

**Render notes** — The tell of a fake garden render: uniformly perfect, uniformly mature, uniformly in flower.
Real gardens have a dominant green mass with a limited flower moment; hierarchy of texture beats a rainbow.
Get the species right for the stated climate (no birches in Dubai, no palms in Berlin). Show the ground plane
honestly — paving with joints, subtle staining, a hose, gravel that has scattered. Shadows from the enclosing
walls are the whole atmosphere of a courtyard; render them. If the brief is day-one handover, plant it at
day-one size and say so; if the image shows the 15-year garden, label it "shown at maturity".

## Public park & urban park

**What drives the design** — Programme and desire lines: parks are shaped by how people actually cross them,
which is rarely how the plan wants them to. Then maintenance capacity — a municipal park is designed to the
budget of the mowing contract, and an ambitious perennial scheme with no gardener reverts to weeds within two
seasons. Topography, existing trees (the most valuable asset on any site — survey and protect them), and
hydrology. Safety and passive surveillance: sightlines, edge treatment, and the fear of the enclosed shrubbery.
Flood function increasingly drives the whole plan — many urban parks are now stormwater infrastructure that
happens to be lawn.

**Key metrics** — Path widths: main shared route 3.0–4.0 m where cycling and walking mix; primary pedestrian
loop 2.0–3.0 m; secondary 1.5–1.8 m; informal 1.2 m. Longitudinal gradient on accessible routes generally kept
to ≤5% (1:20), with steeper sections treated as ramps with landings — verify against the local accessibility
code. Cross-fall on paths ~1–2.5% (enough to shed water, not enough to feel tilted). Open lawn needs a
minimum practical dimension to be usable for informal ball games — indicatively 30 × 30 m and up. Tree
spacing: for a closed canopy avenue, spacing is set by the mature crown, commonly 8–12 m for medium species
and 12–18 m for large ones. Seating: a bench roughly every 50–100 m on an accessible route is a widely used
rule of thumb, with more where the population is older. Shade is the metric nobody records and everyone feels.

**Technical drivers** — SUDS/stormwater as the organising system: swales, rain gardens, detention basins and
permeable surfaces sized by the local rainfall event and infiltration rate (a percolation test, not a guess).
Soil compaction during construction is the number one killer of park planting — specify protected root zones,
no-go areas, and decompaction before planting. Sub-base build-up under paths sized for the maintenance
vehicle, not the pedestrian. Irrigation for establishment. Vandal-resistant and repairable detailing: assume
every fixing will be tested. Lighting only where the park is genuinely used after dark — a lit path through an
unwatched park can feel less safe, not more.

**Planting logic** — Structure first: canopy trees (the 50–100 year layer), then understorey, then the ground
layer that carries the maintenance cost. Diversity is resilience — avoid monocultures; a single-species avenue
is one pathogen away from a clear-fell. Plan for succession: what replaces the canopy when it ages out, and
where are the replacement trees already growing? Match species to the hydrological zone (a rain garden bottom
is periodically inundated; its edge is not). Native and locally-adapted species reduce input and support
fauna, but "native" is not automatically low-maintenance. Meadow and naturalistic planting is a *maintenance
strategy*, not a way of avoiding one — it needs cutting and removal at the right moment each year.

**Common mistakes** — Designing the plan the mowers cannot mow, then blaming the mowers. Shrub massing that
creates hiding places along the only route home. Beautiful paving in a park with no shade, unusable four
months a year. Trees planted into compacted construction subgrade with no decompaction — they survive on
nursery reserves for three years and then decline, and everyone blames the nursery. Ignoring the desire line,
then fencing the resulting dirt track. A single grand gesture that consumes the whole budget, leaving the
90% of the park where people actually are undetailed.

**Render notes** — Convincing parks are *inhabited* and *uneven*. Show people doing different things at
different distances, not a scatter of cutouts walking left. Grass wears — show the path people actually take.
Trees should be a mix of ages and species, with correct mature scale and correct canopy transparency for the
species; a park render with 40 identical instanced trees at identical size reads as CGI instantly. Seasonal
honesty: pick a season and commit to it (bare deciduous branches in a winter render are not a failure, they
are credibility). Ground plane needs texture variation, leaf litter, and shadow dappling — a flat green
shader is the fastest way to lose an audience.

## Plaza / hard public realm

**What drives the design** — Solar orientation and shade — this decides whether the plaza is used or empty,
and it outranks the paving pattern by an enormous margin. Then edges: a plaza is made by what surrounds it,
and a square with dead frontage on three sides is a windswept void no material palette can save. Wind (in
tall-building contexts a wind microclimate study is not optional). Programme — markets, events, service and
emergency vehicle access. Drainage. Subsurface: plazas over car parks, metro boxes and services are the norm,
and the available soil depth is whatever the structure leaves.

**Key metrics** — Gradients: paved public space generally 1–2% fall for drainage; accessible circulation
routes kept at or below 5% (1:20) longitudinally, with anything steeper handled as a ramp with landings;
cross-fall commonly limited to ~2% (verify locally — the exact figures are jurisdictional). A plaza that is
"level" at 0% will pond. Seating: the widely-cited rule of thumb (after Whyte) is roughly **0.3 linear metres
of sittable edge per square metre of open plaza** — treat as indicative, but the underlying point is
non-negotiable: people sit where there is something to sit on. Seat height 400–450 mm; ledges usable at
400–500 mm. Shade target: aim for a meaningful proportion of the usable area in shade at the hottest hour of
the design day — quantify it with a real shadow study, do not eyeball it. Fire/service vehicle access needs
verified loading and turning geometry.

**Technical drivers** — Falls, gullies, slot drains and channel lines coordinated *with* the paving setting-out
so the drainage does not cut the pattern to pieces. Structural build-up over podium/basement (pedestal paving,
drainage layer, waterproofing, root barrier). Tree pits in hard surface are the central technical problem —
see Streetscape. Paving on a rigid vs flexible base, and movement joints. Salt and de-icing regimes destroy
both plants and stone in cold climates. Glare and heat: light paving reduces surface temperature but can
create punishing glare; dark paving in a hot climate becomes a radiant heater.

**Planting logic** — In a hard plaza, trees are infrastructure, not decoration: they deliver the shade that
makes the space work. Give them real soil volume (structural soil, soil cells, or a continuous trench) and
choose species tolerant of reflected heat, compaction, restricted rooting and drought. Cluster trees into
groves rather than dotting them singly — grove canopies close, shade compounds, and root trenches can be
shared. Ground-level planting in a plaza gets trampled, so either protect it properly or don't pretend.

**Common mistakes** — The beautiful, unshaded, unsittable plaza — polished granite, a sculptural bench nobody
can sit on, no canopy, and photographs of it are always empty for a reason. Trees specified with a 1 × 1 m pit
and 1 m³ of soil, then blamed for dying or for lifting the paving. Drainage designed after the paving pattern.
Bollards and clutter defeating the flexibility that was the whole argument for hard surface. Wind tunnels
between towers with seating placed straight into them. Furniture chosen from a catalogue image with no
thought about where the sun is at 2 pm.

**Render notes** — An empty plaza render is an admission of failure — populate it, and populate it with people
doing plaza things (sitting on edges, standing in shade, queueing, eating). Shadow is the subject: get the sun
angle right for the stated latitude, date and hour, and let the shade patches be where people are. Paving needs
joints, slight tonal variation between units, and wear at the entrances; a single seamless stone texture is the
classic tell. Show the drainage lines — real plazas have channels and gullies, and their absence reads as
unreal even to viewers who could not name why. Trees at correct **mature** canopy spread, with shadow to match.

## Streetscape

**What drives the design** — The cross-section: every millimetre is contested between carriageway, parking,
cycle lane, footway, planting and services. Then services — the underground is usually full, and the tree you
want is exactly where the gas main is. Then the maintenance and cleansing regime, salt and pollution loading,
and pedestrian volume. Kerb lines and levels drive drainage. Loading, refuse, and emergency access drive the
things designers pretend they can ignore.

**Key metrics** — Footway: an absolute minimum clear width of ~1.5–1.8 m is a common baseline (a wheelchair and
a pedestrian passing); 2.0 m+ is comfortable, and busy retail streets need considerably more. This clear width
is *clear* — measured past bins, poles, and café tables. Cross-fall on footway ~1–2.5%. **Tree soil volume is
the number that matters:** a widely used rule of thumb is roughly **0.6–1.0 m³ of rootable soil per m² of
projected mature canopy**, which for a large mature street tree lands indicatively in the **15–30 m³** range —
figures vary by source and climate, but a 1 m³ pit is an execution, not a planting. Tree pit surface opening
commonly 1.5–2.0 m across (larger is better); rooting depth of use is typically the top 600–900 mm — trees root
wide, not deep, which is why continuous trenches, structural soil, and soil cells outperform isolated pits.
Clearance: keep branch canopy above the footway at ~2.4–2.5 m and above the carriageway at ~4.5–5.0 m
(jurisdictional — verify).

**Technical drivers** — Root barriers and root-directing detail where the tree is close to kerb, services or
building foundations — but a root barrier without soil volume just relocates the failure. Structural soil / soil
cells / continuous trenches to reconcile rootable volume with the load-bearing pavement above. Irrigation and
aeration for establishment (tree pit irrigation tubes are common and commonly useless if nobody fills them —
design a regime, not a fitting). Passive irrigation from surface runoff into the tree pit is a strong move where
the local rules allow it, but not where runoff is salted. Utilities coordination is the real design work.
De-icing salt, pollution, reflected heat, wind funnelling and dog urine are the actual growing conditions.

**Planting logic** — Street trees are the most hostile plant position in landscape architecture; specify for
the site conditions that exist, not the ones you wish existed. Tolerance of compaction, drought, salt (in cold
climates), pollution and restricted rooting narrows the palette hard. Diversity guards against the next
Dutch-elm/ash-dieback event — a common planning rule of thumb caps any one species at a modest share of the
street population. Match the tree's mature size and form to the street's dimension and the building line: a
large-canopy species under a 4-storey facade with a 2 m footway is a topping contract waiting to happen. Choose
species with a clear stem habit for sightlines and vehicle clearance.

**Common mistakes** — Specifying trees with no root volume, then acting surprised when they die at year 7 or
heave and crack the paving they were strangled by. A "clear" footway with a lighting column, a bin and a
signpost down the middle of it. Ornamental planting beds on a street with no maintenance contract, which become
litter traps. Choosing a species for its flower and inheriting a fruit that stains the paving and a slip claim.
Setting kerb levels before the drainage. Building a cycle lane that ends at the tree.

**Render notes** — Streets are the easiest place to spot a fake: get the tree scale honest — a newly planted
street tree has a 3–4 m stem and a thin crown, and rendering it as a 12 m spreading specimen on completion day
is *the* classic tell. Say which it is. Include the real furniture (bins, signage, columns, gullies, drainage
channels, tactile paving, dropped kerbs, utility covers) — their absence is uncanny. Kerbs need a real profile
and shadow. Show wear: kerb chips, tyre marks, patched asphalt. People at multiple depths, and vehicles if the
street has them.

## Roof garden / green roof

**What drives the design** — Structure. The building's spare load capacity decides the entire typology before a
single plant is chosen, and it is a conversation with the structural engineer at concept stage, not a
coordination item at tender. Then waterproofing and the leak risk — the cost of a failure on a roof is orders of
magnitude greater than on grade. Then wind exposure, sun and reflected heat, access (for people, for
maintenance, for replacement soil and plants), and irrigation. Then maintenance: nobody goes up there, which is
why so many green roofs are a brown crust within five years.

**Key metrics** — Substrate depth is the defining split:
- **Extensive** — indicatively **60–150 mm** substrate; sedum/moss/herb; low or no irrigation once established;
  minimal access; saturated load commonly quoted around **~1.0–1.5 kN/m²** at these depths (indicative — get the
  actual figure from the system supplier and the engineer).
- **Semi-intensive** — indicatively **120–250 mm**; grasses, herbs, small shrubs; periodic irrigation.
- **Intensive** (a roof *garden*, walkable and planted) — indicatively **200 mm+** for lawn and perennials,
  **400–600 mm+** for shrubs, and **≥1.0 m** (often considerably more, plus a much wider rooting area) for small
  trees; saturated loads run to several kN/m² and rise steeply with depth.
Saturated substrate weighs roughly **1,200–1,800 kg/m³** depending on mix — always design on the **saturated**
weight plus imposed live load, never dry weight. Falls to outlets and a secondary/overflow drainage path are
mandatory. Parapet/balustrade heights and wind loads are code items.

**Technical drivers** — The stack, in order: structural deck → waterproofing membrane (electronically testable
where possible) → **root barrier** → protection layer → drainage/reservoir layer → filter fleece → substrate →
planting. Every one of these is load-bearing on the outcome. Leak-detection and access to outlets and inspection
chambers must be designed in and reachable *after* planting. Wind uplift on exposed roofs governs substrate
retention at edges, tree anchoring (guy below grade or root-ball anchor systems — a staked tree on a roof is a
lever), and plant desiccation. Irrigation on an intensive roof is not optional in most climates. Fire breaks and
non-combustible margins at parapets/roof lights are code items in many jurisdictions. Drainage must cope with
the substrate at field capacity during a design storm.

**Planting logic** — Rooftops are hotter, brighter, windier and drier than the ground five metres below, with a
finite root volume that can never expand. Choose plants for exposure, wind and drought — coastal, alpine, steppe
and prairie palettes work; woodland palettes do not. Extensive roofs are a sedum-and-herb ecology, and asking
them to be a meadow at 80 mm of substrate is a fantasy. Trees on roofs need a substrate volume, an anchoring
system and an irrigation regime that were all engineered, and their mature size must be capped by species choice
rather than by pruning. Biodiverse/"brown" roofs with varied substrate depth and aggregate outperform uniform
sedum mats ecologically, and read better too.

**Common mistakes** — Designing the green roof without the structural engineer, then value-engineering it into
100 mm of sedum. Calculating loads on dry substrate. Trees on a roof with the root volume of a large planter and
a stake instead of an anchor. No irrigation and no maintenance access — the two failures always found together.
No root barrier over the membrane. Outlets buried under substrate and planting so the first blocked gully is
discovered by the tenant below. A roof garden rendered as a lawn on a slab with no thought about how the water
gets out or how the mower gets up.

**Render notes** — Roofs are windy and bright, and the render should look it — grasses moving, thin lean crowns
on trees, hard high-contrast light. Substrate depth must be *believable*: a 6 m tree growing out of what is
clearly a 200 mm build-up is instantly wrong to any landscape architect looking at the image. Show the
build-up's consequences — a raised planter edge, a step up, a gravel margin at the parapet, an outlet, a paving
pedestal shadow line. Species must be exposure-appropriate; a rooftop render full of shade-loving woodland
perennials is a lie. Include the parapet/balustrade honestly at code height — renders that quietly delete the
balustrade to protect the view are a known cheat.

## Water features

**What drives the design** — Maintenance and access, which decide whether the feature is running in year three
or drained and full of leaves. Then water source, water loss (evaporation, splash, wind carry, leakage), water
treatment, and where the plant room goes. Then safety — depth, edge, and whether children will get in, because
they will. Then climate: a water feature in a freezing climate must be drainable and freeze-tolerant, and in a
hot dry climate it is an evaporation liability that also happens to be the best cooling device available. Then
sound, which is often the actual brief.

**Key metrics** — Shallow reflecting pools are typically very shallow (indicatively in the **50–150 mm** range)
— enough to reflect, little enough to be safe and to be cleaned; a dark base and a still surface do the
reflecting, not the depth. Wind carries spray: a common rule of thumb is to keep jet height below roughly the
distance from the jet to the pool edge, and to fit a wind sensor that cuts the jets above a threshold — verify
against the fountain supplier's data. Splash zones and adjacent paving need slip-resistant surfaces and their
own drainage. Depths above a jurisdictional threshold (often low — commonly cited around 300–600 mm, but this
is genuinely local) trigger barrier/fencing and pool-safety regulation: **check the local rule, do not assume**.
Circulation turnover rates, filtration and disinfection are specialist engineering — do not invent numbers.

**Technical drivers** — Waterproofing and structural design of the tank (an engineer signs this off). A plant
room with actual space, actual access and actual drainage — pumps, filters, dosing, balance tank, level control,
and an isolation valve someone can reach. Automatic top-up and overflow. Winterisation: drain-down and frost
protection in cold climates. Water treatment and Legionella risk on any feature producing aerosol — this is a
health regulation, not a design preference. Leaves, algae, and hard-water scale are the daily reality. Power
and control cabling to IP-rated fittings; RCD/GFCI protection. Underwater lighting only if it can be *reached*.

**Planting logic** — Aquatic and marginal planting works in zones by water depth — submerged, floating-leaved,
marginal, damp-edge — and each zone has a depth band that must be built into the tank profile as shelves. Aquatic
planting is a water-quality strategy (nutrient uptake, shading, oxygenation) as much as an aesthetic one, and a
naturalistic pool with no planting will go green. Reflecting pools, by contrast, want *nothing* in them —
reflection needs a still, dark, empty surface. Avoid overhanging deciduous trees directly above open water unless
someone is being paid to net and clear it every autumn.

**Common mistakes** — Water features with no maintenance access: no plant room, no drain-down, no way to reach
the underwater lights, and no budget for the cleaning. Jets sized for a still day, drenching the pavement every
windy afternoon. Naturalistic pools with no planted zone and no shade, which turn to algae soup by July.
Reflecting pools built too deep, so they are a safety and cleaning problem *and* they reflect no better.
Underwater lighting aimed straight at the viewer. Specifying moving water in a drought-stressed city without
being able to answer the water-consumption question. Assuming "it recirculates, so it uses no water" —
evaporation and splash losses are real and continuous.

**Render notes** — Water is where renders most often break. Reflections must be *consistent with the scene* —
a mirrored sky that does not match the rendered sky is instantly wrong. Still water reflects and needs almost no
surface detail; moving water needs correct droplet scale and appropriate motion blur/shutter for the stated
camera. Wet edges: paving darkens where it is splashed, and that dark ring is a huge realism win. Spray drifts
downwind, not symmetrically. Show the practicalities — a grille, a weir line, a coping detail. And render the
water at plausible clarity: crystal-clear water over a spotless base is a swimming-pool look, not a public
fountain look.

## Landscape lighting (shared)

**The principle** — Light reveals; it does not illuminate everything. The eye adapts to the darkest thing it can
see, so a single over-bright fitting destroys a whole scheme. Design the *dark* as carefully as the light, and
always ask what the fitting looks like *to a person standing in the space*, not on the plan.

**Layers** — Four, used together:
1. **Path / functional** — low-level, shielded, for safe movement. Bollards, step lights, handrail-integrated
   lighting, low downlights from structure. Even, low, glare-free. Do not line a path with bright bollards
   spaced like runway markers; light the *surface*, not the fitting.
2. **Ambient / orientation** — soft general wash establishing the space's shape and edges: light on a wall, a
   hedge face, a canopy underside. This is what makes a place feel safe, not brightness.
3. **Accent** — selective, higher-contrast light on chosen objects: a specimen tree, a sculpture, a gate.
   Contrast ratio does the work; if everything is accented, nothing is.
4. **Feature / effect** — moonlighting (fittings high in a tree casting dappled shadow down), grazing across
   texture, silhouetting an object against a lit surface, underwater fittings.

**Temperature and colour** — Warm is the default outdoors: indicatively **2700 K** for intimate/domestic and
amenity spaces, **3000 K** for general public realm. Cooler sources (≥4000 K) look clinical, wash foliage grey,
and carry the highest ecological and sky-glow penalty. Foliage generally renders best under warm-to-neutral
sources; some designers use a slightly cooler source *within* foliage to keep green from going yellow — but
verify on site, not in a catalogue. Keep the temperature consistent within a scheme; mixed colour temperatures
across a garden are the most common amateur tell and the most common thing a client complains about without
knowing why.

**Glare control** — Non-negotiable. Shield, recess, baffle, louvre, honeycomb, snoot, and aim *away* from the
eye. Never place an unshielded uplight at the centre of a viewing position. Mount fittings so the source is not
visible from normal sightlines. Uplights set flush in paving are stepped on, filled with grit, and shine
straight into the faces of the people you are lighting the path for — use them sparingly, shielded, and
off-route. As a rule: **if you can see the lamp, the design has failed.**

**Dark-sky / ecological** — Upward light is wasted light. Keep upward light output as close to zero as the
concept allows; use full-cutoff fittings for functional lighting; cap lumen output rather than defaulting to
"more". Artificial light at night disrupts insects, bats, birds and nocturnal mammals; warmer colour
temperatures and lower intensities reduce (do not eliminate) the harm. Curfews and time-controlled dimming
after a set hour are a serious and cheap intervention. Near sensitive habitat, water bodies and dark corridors,
the right answer is often no light at all.

**Uplighting trees vs washing walls** — Uplighting a tree reveals *structure*: trunk, branching, canopy
underside. Aim from outside the drip line where possible, use narrow beams for a strong trunk and wider for the
canopy, and place fittings so the beam does not exit the canopy into the sky or into someone's bedroom window.
Two fittings at an angle read better than one head-on. Evergreens absorb light and need more; open deciduous
crowns need much less than people assume. Washing a wall, by contrast, reveals *plane and texture* — grazing at
a shallow angle exaggerates texture (superb on stone, cruel on flat render), while washing from further out
gives a smooth even field that becomes the background against which planting reads in silhouette. Use lit
vertical surfaces as your ambient layer, and let the trees be the accent.

**Render notes** — Night landscape renders fail by being *too bright and too even*. Real night light is
high-contrast, pooled, and falls off fast — let the darks be dark. Keep the colour temperature consistent and
warm, add the slight haze that makes beams and atmosphere legible, and show fittings' *effects* rather than
glaring sources. Include the ambient sources a real site has: windows, a distant street, sky glow. A tree
uplit in a render should show the beam's fall-off up the trunk and the canopy going dark at the top, not a
uniformly bright plastic tree. If a fitting is visible as a bright dot, you have rendered the mistake, not the
design.
