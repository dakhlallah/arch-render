# Typology Briefs — Healthcare
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Figures are widely-used rules of thumb for design development.
> Healthcare is one of the most heavily regulated typologies — every figure here MUST be verified against
> the governing local code and the client's own health authority standards before design freeze.
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

> **Every figure below is either source-tagged or stated qualitatively.** An untagged number is a bug.
> Healthcare is the most regulated typology there is — verify EVERY figure against the governing code
> and the client's health-authority standards. Nothing here substitutes for a healthcare planner.

Reference frameworks assumed below: FGI *Guidelines for Design and Construction* (US) `[FGI]`; NHS HBN / HTM (UK) `[HBN/HTM]`; Neufert `[Neufert]` and *Architectural Graphic Standards* `[AGS]`; IBC Group I-2 `[IBC]` and NFPA 101 `[NFPA 101]`; ASHRAE 170 *Ventilation of Health Care Facilities* `[ASHRAE 170]`; ISO 14644 cleanroom classes `[ISO 14644]`; national biosafety regs (WHO / CDC-BMBL / EU) `[BMBL]`; accessibility `[ADA]`; otherwise `[common practice]`. These frameworks **disagree materially** on room sizes, clearances and air changes — pick the one that governs and never mix them in one drawing set.

## THE FIVE FLOWS — the heart of the typology
Every healthcare plan is judged on whether **five flows can be kept separable**. If you cannot draw all five on the plan in five colours without them merging, the plan is not finished. This is the first thing a healthcare planner looks for and the first thing that fails peer review.

1. **Public / outpatient** — visitors, ambulant patients, families. Front door, waiting, public lifts.
2. **Inpatient / bed** — trolleys, beds, patients under care. Sets corridor width and lift car size.
3. **Staff** — clinicians moving between clinical zones without passing through public space.
4. **Clean supply** — sterile instruments, linen, drugs, food, consumables from CSSD / stores.
5. **Soiled / waste** — used instruments, soiled linen, clinical waste, specimens, mortuary.

**NEVER cross:**
- **soiled/waste × clean/sterile supply** — the cardinal sin. Infection control will reject the plan.
- **mortuary / deceased route × any public route.**
- **ambulance/ED route × visitor drop-off** — they will physically block each other on the worst day of the year, which is the only day it matters.
- **sterile OR corridor × general staff or visitor traffic.**
- **a distressed, aggressive or infectious patient × the general waiting room.**
- **goods-in / loading dock × a public corridor** — service must never surface front-of-house.

## DEFEND IN PLACE — the life-safety concept that governs everything
**Inpatients cannot self-evacuate.** An anaesthetised patient, a patient in traction, a ventilated ICU patient and a woman mid-caesarean do not use a stair. So institutional healthcare occupancies are designed to **defend in place**: the fire is contained and patients are moved **horizontally**, through a smoke barrier, into the **adjacent smoke compartment on the same floor** — which becomes their refuge. Vertical egress is a last resort, not the strategy. Consequences you design for, rather than discover:

- The floor plate is subdivided into **smoke compartments** — under IBC I-2, max **22,500 sf (≈2,090 m²)** per compartment and max **200 ft (≈61 m)** travel to a smoke barrier door `[IBC]` `[NFPA 101]`.
- Each compartment must hold its neighbour's patients: refuge area of **≥30 sf (≈2.8 m²) per patient** on **each** side of the barrier `[IBC]` — that is bed-sized area you must find inside the plan.
- **Every smoke compartment needs at least two exits**, and every smoke-barrier door must pass a bed.
- Fully sprinklered; hazardous areas (soiled utility, bulk store, plant) separately rated `[IBC]`.
- Essential power in branches — life-safety, critical, equipment `[NFPA 101]`. A hospital that goes dark kills people; the generator, fuel store and its cooling take real plan area, not a plant symbol.
- The smoke-barrier layout is fixed **early** — it constrains the structural grid, riser positions and where you may put an atrium. Retrofitting one into a finished plan is brutal.

## IMAGING IS EQUIPMENT-DICTATED — get the vendor drawing before you fix structure
Imaging rooms are not architectural rooms that happen to contain a machine; they are **the machine, with a room built to its manufacturer's drawing.** You do not size them, the vendor does — and the vendor's siting drawing must be in your hands **before** the structural grid, the slab thickness, the floor-to-floor and the routes are frozen. There is no recovering from this later.

- **MRI** — the magnet's **5-gauss line** is an exclusion zone that **passes through walls, floors and ceilings**, and it is *magnet-specific*: **never assume a distance — read it off the vendor's field plot.** Nothing ferrous, no pacemaker, no corridor, no lift, no store, no car park bay, no plant inside it. The room is an **RF cage** (a continuous conductive shell with a waveguide penetration panel and a shielded door), it needs a **quench pipe to outside**, and the magnet is heavy and has to physically get in — the **delivery route is a design constraint on the whole building**, and on some projects the magnet goes in before the façade does.
- **CT / linear accelerator / X-ray** — **shielding thickness (lead, concrete, or both) comes from the radiation physicist's shielding report, and from nowhere else.** It depends on the machine, the workload, the occupancy of the space next door and above and below. A linac bunker is a civil-engineering object with metre-thick walls and a maze entry, and it belongs at grade or below.
- Consequences: imaging is heavy, low, and hard to move; it wants to be **adjacent to the ED and the OR** (the hot triangle); and it is the equipment most likely to be *replaced within the building's life* — so give every imaging room a **replacement route** (a knock-out panel, a wide corridor, a route to a dock) or you will be taking a wall down in fifteen years.

## Hospital (acute / inpatient)
**Program ratios** — Net-to-departmental-gross in clinical departments runs roughly **1.4–1.6×**, and departmental-gross to building-gross adds a further **~1.3–1.5×** `[common practice]` — so usable clinical net is typically only about **half** of building gross. Circulation **~25–35%** of gross; plant, shafts and interstitial services a further **~10–20%** in a modern acute hospital (OR HVAC, imaging, medical gases, IT) `[common practice]`. Inpatient bedrooms are a *minority* of the area: the diagnostic & treatment (D&T) block and its support drive the plan.
**Key metrics** — Whole-hospital gross per bed is quoted very widely — from about **90 m² to well over 150 m²/bed (≈1,000–1,600+ sf/bed)**, rising sharply for tertiary/academic work `[common practice]`. Treat it as a sanity check, never a brief. Floor-to-floor: **~3.9–4.2 m** typical in ward blocks, **~4.5–5.0 m** where OR and imaging sit, to carry a **1.5–2.0 m** structure-to-finished-ceiling services zone `[common practice]`. Fix floor-to-floor before the OR/imaging services are known and you have already lost the ceiling void.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Group I-2 or local equivalent: **defend-in-place, smoke compartmentation, horizontal evacuation** (see above) `[IBC]` `[NFPA 101]`. Corridor width is set by **bed movement**, not occupant load. **Ventilation is a code matter, not a comfort matter** — pressure relationship, air-change rate, filtration and temperature/RH band are *prescribed per room type*: OR **positive**; airborne-infection isolation (AII) **negative, with an anteroom**; protective-environment (immunosuppressed) rooms **positive with HEPA** `[ASHRAE 170]`. **Take the air-change rate, filtration efficiency and pressure differential for every room type from the current ASHRAE 170 table (or HTM 03-01 / the local health authority) — never from memory, and never from the last project.** Full accessibility throughout `[ADA]`.
**Spatial standards** — Single inpatient room: **200 sf (≈18.6 m²) clear floor area**, exclusive of toilet, vestibule and wardrobe `[FGI]`; HBN 04-01 single en-suite rooms are dimensioned differently — confirm which standard governs before you draw one `[HBN/HTM]`. Clearance **≥3 ft (0.9 m)** at each side and the foot of the bed `[FGI]`. Corridors in inpatient/bed-movement zones: **8 ft (2.44 m) clear** so two beds pass `[FGI]`. Lifts: at least one **bed/trolley lift** whose car takes a bed *plus* an attendant at each end *plus* an IV pole — size it from the client's actual bed, not a catalogue.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — The **five flows** (above) govern everything. The **"hot triangle" — OR · ICU · imaging —** is the plan's spine: same level, or a dedicated lift bank serving only them, with the ED plugged straight in. Materials management (dock, CSSD, kitchen, waste) gets its own service core that never surfaces front-of-house. Wards go where daylight and quiet are; D&T goes where the services and the loading are.
**Common mistakes** — Sizing the hospital from bed count alone and under-providing D&T and plant. Losing the ceiling void to duct coordination. A single-loaded ward with no back-of-house route, so soiled linen travels the public corridor. Putting a corridor, lift or store inside the MRI fringe field. Letting the ED entrance compete with the main entrance for kerb. And the big one: **treating expansion as a façade problem — it is the D&T block, not the ward tower, that grows.** Give D&T a *soft edge* to grow into, or you are designing the hospital's ceiling on day one.
**Render notes** — A convincing hospital render is **calm, bright, deep-plan and low-drama**: high-CRI diffuse ceiling light, daylight from a courtyard or ward window, matte non-glossy floors (sheet vinyl / linoleum with **coved skirting**, not polished stone), washable wall finishes, handrails and crash rails, flush cleanable detailing with **no dust ledges**. Include the real equipment: the **bed-head service panel** (gases + power + nurse call), IV pole, sanitiser dispensers, clinical hand-wash basin with lever/sensor tap, door vision panels, ceiling curtain track. People: staff in scrubs, a porter with a bed, a visitor — not a lone posing model. Fake tells: **mood lighting and dark accent walls — there is no mood lighting in a ward**; heroic double-height glass over a bed; spotless corridors with no signage, no wall protection and no fire doors; furniture that obviously cannot be wiped down; and **no ceiling** — a hospital ceiling is an honest, busy grid of lights, sprinklers, sensors and diffusers, and hiding it is the tell.

## Clinic / Medical centre (ambulatory)
**Program ratios** — The **exam room is the unit of production**. Net-to-gross typically **~0.6–0.7** (gross factor **~1.4–1.6×**) `[common practice]`. Rough split of net: clinical (exam/treatment/procedure) **~40–50%**; staff and clinical support (care-team station, clean & soiled utility, offices, break) **~25–30%**; public (reception, waiting, WC) **~15–25%**; plant/IT modest by acute standards, **~5–8%** of gross `[common practice]`.
**Key metrics** — Commonly **~2–3 exam rooms per full-time provider** — more for high-throughput primary care, fewer for long-consult specialties `[common practice]`. Whole-clinic gross of roughly **50–90 m² (≈550–1,000 sf) per exam room**, all support and public space included, is a workable indicative band `[common practice]` — the client's own operating model beats any rule of thumb. Waiting seats scale with that operating model, not with room count: an app-check-in clinic needs a fraction of the seats a walk-in one does. **Get the seat count from the throughput model, not from a ratio.**
**Code & compliance drivers** — Ordinary **business occupancy** in most codes — **provided** patients remain capable of self-preservation `[IBC]`. The moment you add sedation, anaesthesia or a recovery bay, the room or suite changes occupancy class and pulls in hospital-grade ventilation, egress and essential power: one line on the brief can change the whole building. Accessibility is the dominant daily driver — accessible route to every patient-facing room, a **1,500 mm (60 in) turning circle** and clear floor space beside the exam table `[ADA]`, a lowered reception counter section. Procedure rooms bring ventilation and washable-finish requirements `[ASHRAE 170]`. Sharps, clinical waste and lockable (sometimes controlled-substance-rated) medication storage are all regulated.
**Spatial standards** — Standard exam room: **120 sf (≈11.2 m²) clear floor area** `[FGI]` — a common module is ~3.0 × 3.7 m. Consult (talk-only) room **~9–12 m²** `[common practice]`. Corridors in a clinic with no bed movement: **6 ft (1.83 m) clear** in outpatient areas `[FGI]`, wider if stretchers are used. Reception counter dual-height: standing **~1.0 m**, accessible section **~0.75–0.8 m** `[AGS]` `[ADA]`.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — The typology hinges on the **on-stage / off-stage split**: the patient enters the exam room from a public corridor, staff enter the *same* room from a back corridor, with the care-team station in the middle. Get that and everything follows; miss it and staff and patients collide all day. Waiting has a direct sightline to reception but **not** into the clinical corridor. Phlebotomy and imaging near the entry (patients often come for these alone). Clean utility upstream of exam rooms, soiled utility downstream by the back exit. Provide at least one **isolation / direct-entry room near the front door** so an infectious or distressed patient never crosses the waiting room. The staff break room should be genuinely invisible to patients.
**Common mistakes** — A single-corridor clinic, and then puzzlement at the collisions. A grand waiting room and an afterthought staff core — the staff core is where the day is won. **Mirroring exam rooms left/right: keep them same-handed**, because clinicians run on muscle memory and a mirrored room slows every encounter. Forgetting the sub-wait / gowned-patient waiting for imaging and specialty. Clean and soiled utility reduced to two doors and a shelf.
**Render notes** — The credible clinic is **daylit, low-institutional, warm without being a hotel lobby**: soft indirect + linear ceiling light, resilient flooring (wood-look or terrazzo-look), **seating with arms** (patients need arms to stand up), a genuinely accessible reception counter, legible signage. Details that sell it: sanitiser at the door, a clinical basin, a wall-mounted otoscope/BP unit in the exam room, a coat hook, a stool on castors, paper roll on the exam table. Fake tells: a cavernous white lobby with no desk; an exam room with no basin and no wall-mounted equipment; glass walls onto clinical space (privacy is a code issue and a decency issue); zero signage; and boutique-hotel lighting in a place where someone has to read a chart.

## Emergency Department
The highest-stakes plan in the building. It runs at 3 a.m. with a half-strength team, and it is the one department that **cannot control its own demand**.
**Program ratios** — Treatment (resus, majors, minors, paeds, mental-health) is only about **half** of the ED's net area; the rest is triage/reception, staff base, clean and soiled utility, imaging, stores, and the observation/short-stay unit that every busy ED grows `[common practice]`. Budget generously for **circulation around trolleys** — an ED corridor is a working space, not a link.
**Key metrics** — **Do not size an ED from an annual-attendance rule of thumb.** Size it from the client's **arrivals model**: peak-hour arrivals × average length of stay, per acuity stream, gives the simultaneous treatment spaces required, plus a resilience margin agreed with the health authority. Anything else designs for the average day and fails on the bad one. Room sizes come from the standard, not from memory — **a resuscitation bay is materially larger than a treatment bay**, because a full trauma team plus a mobile X-ray must work all the way round the trolley; take the clear-area figures from FGI / HBN 15-01 `[FGI]` `[HBN/HTM]`.
**Code & compliance drivers** — I-2 / defend-in-place applies on the treatment side `[IBC]`. Ventilation per room type `[ASHRAE 170]`, and in particular **at least one negative-pressure AII room with an anteroom on the front line, reachable before the patient enters the waiting room** — the pandemic lesson, now embedded in most health-authority standards. **Decontamination**: an externally-accessible decon area with its own entrance, drainage and containment, so a contaminated patient never enters the department. Mental-health assessment room: **anti-ligature fittings, two doors so staff are never trapped, no blind corners** — a safety requirement with a body count, not a fit-out choice. Essential power on every resus bay `[NFPA 101]`.
**Spatial standards** — Ambulance bay: **covered, level, and deep enough to unload a trolley in the rain with the doors open** — dimension it from the client's actual ambulance fleet, not a generic bay. Corridors carry trolleys, so the **bed-movement width applies: 8 ft (2.44 m) clear** `[FGI]`. Resus bays are normally open to the department with curtain or glazed screening for supervision, and the **central staff base must have line of sight to every acute bay**. A dedicated ED X-ray/CT, a bariatric bay and a plaster/procedure room are not optional extras.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — **Two front doors, always — ambulance and walk-in, separated, both arriving at a single point of triage.** From triage the plan **streams by acuity**: resus (straight in, shortest possible route, adjacent to the ambulance door), majors, minors/urgent care (often a separate loop entirely), paeds (separate waiting, separate sightlines), mental health (quiet, safe, supervised, off the main corridor). The ED must plug directly into the **hot triangle**: a **direct, uninterrupted route to CT, the OR and the ICU** that never passes through public space. If the CT is a lift ride away across the main lobby, the plan is wrong. NEVER cross: an arriving trauma **×** the walk-in waiting room · the police/custody or mental-health route **×** paediatric waiting · decon **×** anything.
**Common mistakes** — Designing the ED for the average day. One shared front door for ambulance and walk-in. A resus bay you cannot reach from the ambulance bay in a straight line with a trolley. A staff base with a blind spot. Routing minors through the majors corridor, so a child with a sprained wrist watches a resuscitation. Omitting the observation/short-stay unit, which then colonises the corridor. And forgetting that **the ED is the hospital's real front door**, then detailing it like a back one.
**Render notes** — An ED render is **not** a serene lobby; it reads true when it looks **operational under load**. Exterior: the ambulance canopy at dusk, bay lit, a vehicle backed in, and **wayfinding a frightened person can read at speed from the kerb**. Interior: the **triage desk visible from the door**, trolleys (not beds), curtain tracks, portable monitors, a patient-status whiteboard or screen at the staff base, a mobile X-ray parked in its alcove, wall protection at trolley height, and clocks — EDs are full of clocks. Light is bright, even and glare-free. People: a triage nurse, a paramedic handing over, someone waiting badly. Fake tells: an empty spotless ED; a resus bay with bare walls and no equipment; no ambulance and no canopy; mood lighting; and a waiting room with no reception and no sightline — an ED whose staff cannot *see* is a fiction.

## Operating Theatre Suite
The OR suite drives the floor-to-floor of the whole block. Decide it early, or the section is a lie.
**Program ratios** — The ORs themselves are typically only about **a third** of the suite; the rest is the support that makes them work — pre-op/holding, PACU (recovery), scrub, sterile core, clean and soiled utility, anaesthesia and equipment stores, staff change and lounge, control `[common practice]`. Plan **more than one PACU bay per OR** (recovery is slower than surgery); take the actual ratio from the client's case mix rather than a generic figure `[common practice]`.
**Key metrics** — General OR: **minimum 400 sf (≈37 m²) clear floor area with a minimum clear dimension of 20 ft (6.1 m)** `[FGI]`; in practice new-build general ORs are planned at **~55–65 m² (600–700 sf)**, and hybrid / imaging-integrated ORs at **70 m²+**, because of booms, robots and the imaging gantry's swing `[common practice]`. **Column-free is not negotiable.** Clear height must take the lights, booms and anaesthesia pendant with the table raised — **~3.0 m clear is normal practice** `[common practice]`, with the actual minimum from FGI / the health authority `[FGI]`. Floor-to-floor **~4.5–5.0 m** over an OR block `[common practice]`: the OR's *plant*, not the OR, sets the section.
**Code & compliance drivers** — **The OR is positively pressurised** relative to everything around it, in a cascade **OR → sterile core → clean corridor → outside** `[ASHRAE 170]`. **The air-change rate, filtration stages and temperature/RH band per room are prescribed — read them from the current ASHRAE 170 table or HTM 03-01, never from memory.** Whether an ISO 14644 class is imposed over the surgical field, and which, is jurisdiction-specific — do not assume one `[ISO 14644]`. Essential and isolated power, and medical gases (O₂, N₂O, medical air, vacuum, AGSS scavenging) are life-safety systems with real riser and plant demand `[NFPA 101]`. Finishes seamless, welded, washable and coved — no ledges, no fabric, no open joints.
**Spatial standards** — Doors must pass **a bed plus a team plus a stack of equipment**, and are usually **sliding** (a swing door pumps air across the pressure cascade every time it opens). Scrub sinks sit **immediately outside** the OR door, sensor or knee-operated, recessed clear of the trolley path. The **sterile core** is a clean spine between ORs that only sterile goods and scrubbed staff enter. **Imaging in a hybrid OR is equipment-dictated: get the vendor's siting drawing before you fix a single structural member.** Gantry swing, floor/ceiling rail loads, and the control and equipment room sizes are the manufacturer's numbers, not yours — and the same rule governs the **MRI (5-gauss line + RF cage)** and CT/linac rooms elsewhere in the D&T block, whose **shielding thickness comes from the radiation physicist's report, never from a table in a brief.**
**Flow & adjacency logic** — The classic diagram: **clean corridor / sterile core on one side, "dirty" disposal corridor on the other, OR in between.** Patient in via pre-op holding; staff in via change — which is a **hard clean/dirty threshold, not a locker room**; sterile instruments in from the sterile core; soiled instruments and waste out to the disposal corridor and down to CSSD (ideally directly below or directly adjacent, with a dedicated clean-up / dirty-down lift pair). Patient out to PACU, which needs **direct, non-public access to ICU** for the cases that go badly. NEVER cross: soiled instruments **×** the sterile core · unchanged staff or visitors **×** the restricted zone · a public corridor **×** anything in the suite.
**Common mistakes** — Sizing the OR and forgetting the boom swing, the C-arm, the robot and the microscope that all arrive later. A column in the OR. Fixing floor-to-floor before the OR HVAC and booms are known. Putting the suite where it can never expand — ORs are the estate's revenue engine, and they *always* grow. Ignoring the CSSD relationship and shipping instruments across the site. Treating staff change as a locker room instead of the clean/dirty threshold it is. Assuming an OR can be daylit like a studio — some take controlled daylight, most do not; ask, never assume.
**Render notes** — An OR render lives or dies on **equipment and light**. Get in: the **surgical light head** (the double-satellite pendant — the single most recognisable object in the room), the **ceiling booms**, anaesthesia and surgical, on articulated arms; the **operating table** (narrow, sectioned, on a column — *not* a bed); the anaesthesia machine; the laparoscopy stack; monitors on booms; the wall control panel; gas outlets; and the **ceiling-mounted laminar-flow canopy** over the table — large, gridded, and the thing amateurs always omit. Surfaces are **seamless and hard**: welded vinyl with coved skirting, washable panel walls, sealed ceiling — no tiles, no gaps. Light is bright, cool and *even*, with the surgical light head as the only hot spot. People in full scrubs, hats, masks, gowns, gloves. Fake tells: a normal suspended ceiling grid; no booms; a hospital *bed* instead of an operating table; a window with a view; mood lighting; and — the classic — an OR the size of a bedroom.

## Laboratory (wet lab / research, incl. containment)
**A lab is a services building with some benches in it.** Design the architecture first and the services after, and you will design it twice.
**Program ratios** — Planned as **lab : lab-support : office/write-up**. A common working split of net: **lab ~50%**, **lab support ~20–25%** (tissue culture, cold rooms, instrument rooms, autoclave, glasswash, chemical store), **office/write-up ~25–30%** `[common practice]` — support rises steeply for biology and chemistry, falls for dry/computational labs. Net-to-gross is poor: expect **~0.55–0.65** (gross factor **~1.5–1.8×**) `[common practice]`. Plant is the headline: **~20–30% of gross** for MEP is normal, because lab air is **100% outside air, once-through** — a full plant floor or roof deck is the rule, not the exception.
**Key metrics** — The planning module is near-universal: a **~3.0–3.3 m (10–11 ft) lab module**, benches **~1.5–1.6 m** long, and a clear aisle between opposing benches of **~1.5 m minimum, ~1.8 m preferred** where a fume hood faces a bench `[common practice]` `[AGS]`. Bench depth **~750 mm**, island benches ~1.5 m back-to-back `[Neufert]` `[AGS]`. Floor-to-floor **~4.2–4.8 m** with a **~1.5 m+** ceiling services zone `[common practice]`. Write-up **~4.5–6.0 m² per researcher** in a shared zone `[common practice]`. Structure carries **high live load and low vibration** — and for sensitive instruments (confocal, EM, NMR) it is the **vibration criterion (VC curve), not the load**, that governs: **get the criterion from the instrument vendor, never from a rule of thumb.** Count the fume hoods early — the hood count sets the plant, and therefore the building.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Two overlapping regimes.
- **Chemical** — hazardous-material quantity limits *per control area* decide whether the lab is an ordinary occupancy or a **High-Hazard (H) occupancy**, and the permitted quantity **falls as you go up the building** — which alone can dictate which lab goes on which floor `[IBC]`. Flammable-liquid cabinets, cylinder storage, secondary containment, spill control. Emergency shower + eyewash reachable **within 10 seconds and ~55 ft (16.8 m), unobstructed, on the same level** `[ANSI Z358.1]`.
- **Biological** — containment level **BSL / CL 1–4** `[BMBL]`. The concept in one line: **as the level rises, air moves inwards and never comes back out untreated.** BSL-2 adds restricted access, a biosafety cabinet and washable surfaces. **BSL-3 adds directional inward airflow, an anteroom/airlock, sealed penetrations, HEPA-filtered exhaust and a fully sealable room** — effectively a box within a box, and it must be *drawn* as one from concept, not fitted out later. BSL-4 is a specialist building type, not a room. **The pressure differentials, air-change rates and containment verification tests come from the biosafety manual and the client's biosafety officer — never from memory, and never from another lab.**
- Lab air is **not recirculated**. Fume-hood face velocity is commonly specified around **0.4–0.5 m/s (80–100 fpm)** `[ANSI Z9.5]` `[common practice]` — but the project's number is the safety officer's to give.
- Two means of egress from any lab with a hood or hazardous materials, in most jurisdictions `[IBC]`. And **a lab is a workplace under the accessibility code**: a portion of benching must be height-adjustable with knee space `[ADA]`.
**Spatial standards** — Fume hood **~1.2–1.8 m wide** typical, with **≥1.0–1.2 m clear** in front of the sash `[common practice]`. A hood must **NOT** sit beside a door, under a supply diffuser, or in a circulation path — a cross-draught defeats it. Corridors **≥1.5–1.8 m** `[common practice]`; if the corridor will become an equipment gallery (it always does), widen it **deliberately** rather than letting it happen to you.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — The organising diagram is a **ladder**: a clean office/write-up spine, a lab spine, and a lab-support/service spine — with shared equipment and cold/hot rooms **banded between labs, not inside them** (noise, heat, contamination). **Write-up is glazed-adjacent to, but physically outside, the wet lab**: PPE off, no food, no laptops in the lab. Autoclave and glasswash sit on the service corridor beside the waste route; goods-in, gas store and chemical store connect to the dock without ever passing an office. Vertically: heavy and vibration-sensitive low (grade or basement), H-occupancy chemistry low, and the higher the floor the tighter the hazardous-materials limit. NEVER cross: PPE-on lab traffic **×** the office/social route · biological waste **×** goods-in · a cross-draught **×** a fume-hood face.
**Common mistakes** — Planning the labs and *then* looking for somewhere to put the plant — the shaft and riser strategy is a day-one drawing. Under-counting lab support: the bench gets the glamour and the −80 freezers end up in the corridor (they always do — so **draw a freezer alcove**). A fume hood beside the door. Write-up as desks at the end of the bench, against basic lab practice and most safety policy. A confocal on a long-span floor above a plant room. Designing a beautiful open lab and *then* discovering that BSL-2/3 needs a door, an anteroom and a pressure cascade. Assuming lab air can be recirculated to save energy — it cannot.
**Render notes** — A believable lab is **bright, hard and dense with real equipment** — labs are *over-lit*, never moody. Matte epoxy or welded sheet-vinyl floor with **coved skirting**, sealed washable ceiling (or exposed but sealed services), black phenolic-resin bench tops, mobile under-bench pedestals on castors, service spines and booms with gas taps and power. The giveaways that make it read as real: a **fume hood with the sash partly open and the airflow monitor visible**, an emergency shower + eyewash with its signage, a sharps bin, a **gas cylinder strapped to the wall**, a −80 freezer, a benchtop centrifuge, pipettes in a rack, a biohazard sign on the containment door, a biosafety cabinet behind it. People in lab coats, gloves and **safety glasses — anyone in a wet lab without eye protection destroys the image instantly for a scientist client**. Fake tells: glossy white sci-fi surfaces with nothing on them; blue accent lighting; a lab with no fume hood, no basin and no waste bins; open beakers of coloured liquid; a laptop and a coffee on the bench; and a bare ceiling — a real lab ceiling is a thicket of exhaust duct, sprinklers, gas lines and light.

## WHERE THE REAL NUMBERS COME FROM — never from memory
The figures this brief deliberately **refuses to give**, because giving them from memory is how people get hurt. Each one has an owner. Go and get it from them.

| Figure | Owner / source |
|---|---|
| Air-change rate, filtration stage, pressure differential, temp/RH band — **per room type** | **ASHRAE 170** current table, or **HTM 03-01** / the local health authority |
| Which **ISO 14644** class (if any) applies over the surgical field | The governing jurisdiction / health authority — do **not** assume one |
| **MRI 5-gauss line** distance and field plot | The **magnet vendor's siting drawing** — magnet-specific, never generic |
| **CT / linac / X-ray shielding thickness** | The **radiation physicist's shielding report** — workload- and adjacency-specific |
| **ED size** (number of treatment spaces) | The client's **arrivals model**: peak-hour arrivals × LOS per acuity stream — never an annual-attendance rule of thumb |
| **ED resus / trauma bay** clear areas | **FGI** / **HBN 15-01** — a resus bay is much larger than a treatment bay |
| **BSL-3+** pressure differentials, ACH, verification tests | The **biosafety manual** (BMBL/WHO/local) and the client's **biosafety officer** |
| **Vibration criterion (VC curve)** for a sensitive instrument | The **instrument vendor** — it governs the structure, not the live load |
| **Fume-hood face velocity** for this project | The **safety officer** (a common band is quoted above; the project's number is theirs) |
| **Clinic waiting-seat count** | The client's **throughput / check-in model**, not a seats-per-room ratio |
