# Typology Briefs — Workplace & Retail
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Widely-used rules of thumb for design development — verify
> against local code, and note that workplace density/efficiency benchmarks vary sharply by market and
> by client standard. **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

---

## Office / Corporate HQ
**Program ratios** — Net-to-gross (NIA/GIA) efficiency of a good speculative floorplate lands ~80–85%; below ~75% the scheme is usually over-cored or over-circulated. Core (lifts, stairs, risers, WCs, plant) typically 10–18% of GIA — the low end for shallow suburban plans, the high end for towers. Within NIA, a common split is ~60–70% workspace, 15–25% meeting/collaboration, 10–15% support (print, tea point, lockers, store); internal circulation within NIA usually 20–25% and should be counted, not discovered late. Corporate HQs skew to more meeting and amenity than a spec floorplate does.
**Key metrics** — Planning grid 1.5 m (UK/Europe) or 1.35 m; US typically 5 ft (1.52 m); ceiling/service module 600 mm. Structural grid commonly 6.0/7.5/9.0 m (multiples of the planning grid). Workstation density: allow ~8–10 m²/workstation NIA for planning, with actual occupied density often 10–14 m²/person after hybrid ratios; BCO-type guidance sizes *services and escape* for a denser design case (often 1:8 m²) than the day-one occupancy — set both numbers explicitly with the client. Daylight: usable penetration ≈ 2–2.5× window head height, so 6–7.5 m single-aspect, 12–15 m glass-to-glass double-aspect. Beyond ~18 m depth an atrium or lightwell is effectively mandatory. Floor-to-floor 3.6–4.2 m; clear ceiling 2.7–3.0 m; raised floor 100–200 mm; ceiling/services void 350–600 mm.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load: IBC "Business" gross factor is 150 ft² (13.9 m²) in current editions but was 100 ft² (9.3 m²) in older ones — verify the adopted edition, it swings egress width by ~50%. Two independent means of escape from the floorplate for anything but the smallest floor; travel distance and dead-end corridor limits (commonly 6 m unsprinklered / 15 m sprinklered — confirm locally) drive core position more than any aesthetic argument. Accessible route, 1.5 m turning circles, accessible WC per floor; sanitary provision to BS 6465 or IBC Table 2902.1 (US business: 1 WC per 25 for the first 50 occupants, then 1:50). Fire compartmentation of core, stairs and risers; smoke control for atria. Ventilation/daylight amenity requirements (and WELL/BREEAM/LEED targets if pursued) shape façade and depth.
**Spatial standards** — Desk 1.6 × 0.8 m typical (1.4 × 0.8 m minimum, bench runs 1.4–1.6 m per person). Back-to-back desk rows need 1.0–1.2 m between chairs; primary circulation 1.5–1.8 m, secondary 1.2 m, minimum accessible corridor ~1.1–1.2 m. Meeting rooms: ~2 m² per seated person plus table — 4-person ≈ 8–12 m², 8-person ≈ 18–24 m², 20-person boardroom ≈ 40–55 m². Focus/phone booth ~1.2–1.5 m²; 1:8–1:15 desks is common practice. Lifts sized on 5-minute handling capacity and waiting interval, not on a per-area guess — but ~1 lift per 4,000–5,000 m² NIA is a sane sketch-stage placeholder.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Arrival: street → threshold → reception/security line → lift lobby → floorplate. The security line is the hinge; put it where it can widen without wrecking the lobby. On-floor: lift lobby → reception/hub → circulation spine → workspace, with meeting and amenity clustered near the core (short trips, acoustically buffered) and open desks pushed to the daylit perimeter. Tea points and print rooms are deliberate collision points — on the spine, not hidden. Keep noisy collaboration off the quiet-focus zones with a physical or acoustic break, never just a graphic on the plan.
**Common mistakes** — Floorplate too deep for daylight, then "solved" with more downlights. Core dumped in the geometric centre by default: it can be right, but a centre core on a small plate kills subdivision — you want two-tenant-per-floor splits with independent escape, WCs and lift access, and a badly placed core makes that impossible. Perimeter given to cellular offices and meeting rooms (the classic client-driven error) so 80% of staff sit in the dark. Forgetting that the escape/services density is denser than the occupied density. Columns landing mid-desk because the structural grid was never reconciled with the planning grid. Ceiling zone under-allowed, so ducts crash into beams and the clear height collapses.
**Render notes** — Convincing workplace renders show *inhabited* work: people at ~40–60% of desks (not 100%, not zero), monitors on and slightly angled, a jacket over a chair, mugs, cable trays, a whiteboard with real marks. Light the space the way it will be lived: high daylight at the perimeter, warm task and pendant light deep in plan, a visible falloff. Show the ceiling honestly — grid, linear luminaires, sprinkler heads, exposed services if that's the scheme. Fake tells: empty sterile floors, perfectly identical unbranded chairs, no clutter anywhere, uniform flat lighting with no glare or shadow, glass with no reflection, and views out that are white voids. A view *through* — desk, glazing, city beyond — sells depth better than any wide angle.

## Coworking
**Program ratios** — Denser and more amenity-heavy than corporate: indicatively ~55–70% revenue space (private offices + dedicated/hot desks), 10–20% meeting rooms and booths, 15–25% lounge/café/event/amenity, plus back-of-house (mail, print, IT, storage, cleaner). In mature operators, private/team suites — not open hot desks — carry most of the revenue, so the suite mix is the business model, not a layout preference. Circulation runs high because the amenity is the product.
**Key metrics** — Indicative 5–9 m² per member (NIA) versus 8–10+ m² in corporate; densification is the whole proposition. Membership-to-desk ratio for flexible/hot-desk products is typically 1.2:1 up to 2:1 or more (indicative, operator-dependent) — never design escape or WCs to the *desk* count. Private suites sized in 2/4/6/8/12-person modules on a fixed grid so they can be recombined. Meeting-room provision ~1 seat per 8–12 desks; phone booths ~1 per 10–15 desks (indicative industry practice). Column-free, demountable-partition-friendly plans matter more than an elegant structural expression.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load is the pressure point: the real density, plus an event space, can push you into an assembly classification (commonly triggered around 50 occupants — verify locally), which changes egress width, door swing, exit signage and sometimes the whole building's fire strategy. Escape from deep private suites must not pass through another tenancy. Demountable partitions still have to respect compartmentation, sprinkler coverage and detector spacing — a suite dropped in later can break both. Sanitary provision at the *real* density, not the corporate default. Accessible desks, accessible booth, accessible WC.
**Spatial standards** — Hot desk 1.2–1.6 m wide bench per person; dedicated desk 1.4–1.6 m with pedestal/locker. Circulation 1.2–1.5 m primary; suite doors off a corridor, not off the lounge. Phone booth ~1.0–1.5 m² footprint, 2.1 m+ high, ventilated (an unventilated booth is unusable within 10 minutes and every operator learns this the hard way). Meeting rooms as per office (~2 m²/seat). Lockers, mail wall and print need real square metres. Café/lounge seating 1.4–2.0 m² per seat.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Arrival → host/community desk → café/lounge (the shop window and the social engine) → circulation loop → progressively quieter zones → private suites at the perimeter or back. Noise gradient must be legible: loud (café, events, phone) → medium (open desks) → quiet (suites, focus). Meeting rooms and booths sit on the boundary between zones, doing acoustic work. Service (deliveries, waste, cleaning) needs its own route; in coworking it is almost always forgotten and ends up crossing the lounge at 9am.
**Common mistakes** — Designing the desk count and ignoring the membership multiplier — WCs, escape, coats and the café are the things that fail first. One big undifferentiated open room with no acoustic gradient. Beautiful phone booths with no ventilation or no power. Private suites with no external glazing or borrowed light (unlettable). No expansion path: a 4-person suite that cannot become a 6-person suite because the grid doesn't allow it. Meeting rooms overprovisioned (they're low-yield floor area) or underprovisioned (members leave). Treating the community desk as a reception counter rather than a working, staffed position.
**Render notes** — Coworking renders live or die on *social texture*: overlapping activity — a call in a booth, two people at the café, someone crossing frame with a laptop. Vary the furniture (that's authentic here, unlike corporate), show plants that look watered rather than styled, show power/USB and cables, show a slightly messy pinboard. Warm, layered lighting with visible pendants and lamps; daylight doing the heavy lifting in the lounge. Fake tells: empty booths, no laptops, a lounge with nobody in it, uniform brand-new furniture in matching colours, and zero acoustic treatment visible when the whole scheme depends on it.

## Retail Store
**Program ratios** — Indicatively 70–80% net sales floor to 20–30% stock and BOH for fashion; luxury runs leaner on stock (often 15–20%) with more clienteling space; supermarkets typically ~65–70% sales area with heavy goods-in/prep behind. BOH is not just stock: it's goods-in, unpack/steam/tag, staff WC and break, cleaner, IT/comms, safe/cash office. Undersizing BOH is the single most common retail plan failure and it degrades the sales floor within a month of trading.
**Key metrics** — The store is measured in sales per m² and rent as a % of turnover, so every square metre of dead floor is a real cost. Frontage-to-depth ideally ~1:2 to 1:3; deeper than ~1:4 and the rear of the store dies without a strong visual pull. Entry "decompression/landing zone" of roughly 1.5 m (5 ft) is where shoppers do *not* register merchandise — don't put your hero product there. Most shoppers turn right on entry (in right-hand-traffic markets), so the right-hand "power wall" carries the statement. Fixture height max reach 1.8–2.0 m; keep mid-floor fixtures ≤ ~1.4–1.5 m so sightlines run to the back wall. Ceiling 3.0–4.5 m typical. Merchandise lighting commonly 750–1,500 lux with accent-to-ambient contrast of ~3:1 to 5:1 (up to ~10:1 on a feature), CRI ≥ 90 for anything where colour matters.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load: IBC "Mercantile" gross factor 60 ft² (5.6 m²) for sales areas and 300 ft² (27.9 m²) for stock/shipping — that ratio is why a big stockroom does *not* blow up your exit width, and why converting stock to sales might. Two means of egress once the load or travel distance demands it; the rear/BOH exit is usually one of them, so it must stay clear (it never does — design it wide and label it). Accessible route through the store, accessible checkout position, accessible fitting room. Shopfront/security shutter compliance and glazing safety. Sprinklers affected by high-piled storage in the stockroom.
**Spatial standards** — Primary circulation aisle 1.5–1.8 m; secondary aisles 0.9–1.2 m; minimum accessible clear width 915 mm (36 in) with an accessible checkout aisle also 915 mm clear. Fitting rooms: standard cubicle from ~0.9 × 1.2 m (tight) with 1.1 × 1.5 m more usable; accessible cubicle needs a 1.5 m clear turning circle plus bench and mirror. Provision indicatively ~1 fitting room per 100–150 m² of fashion sales area — verify against the brand's own standard, which usually exists. Cash wrap counter ~900 mm high with an accessible lowered section (≤ 865 mm); allow queue space *in front of it* that doesn't block the door.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Shopfront → decompression zone → power wall / focal point → a looping path that returns to the till and the exit. Give the customer a reason to reach the back (destination category, fitting rooms, the cash desk) — the loop is what converts depth into sales. Fitting rooms want to be adjacent to the relevant merchandise and *supervisable* from the floor, not hidden behind stock. Goods flow must be completely separate: delivery → goods-in → prep → stockroom → floor replenishment, ideally never crossing the customer path in trading hours. Staff need a line of sight from the till over the floor.
**Common mistakes** — Long narrow plan with the good product at the front and a dead tail. Stockroom sized on plan-fit rather than on stock-turn, then boxes end up on the sales floor. Deliveries routed through the shopfront. Fitting rooms parked in the darkest corner or accessed past the stockroom. Uniform flat lighting across the whole store, which kills any merchandising hierarchy — retail is *contrast*, not brightness. Fixtures too tall mid-floor, so the back wall (your biggest asset) is invisible from the door. Forgetting the queue: a till with no queuing zone jams the entrance every Saturday.
**Render notes** — Full shelves, and *imperfectly* full: a gap where something sold, a hanger turned, a tag catching light. Show the merchandising hierarchy in light — accent pools on the power wall and features, softer ambient in circulation, a bright shopfront reading from the street. Include people mid-browse, a staff member, a bag, a reflection in the glazing. Get the shopfront view right: it's the client's actual product. Fake tells: uniformly lit interiors with no accenting, empty fixtures or three lonely garments, no signage/POS/price tickets, no security tags, spotless floors with no scuff, and mannequins that look like mannequins rather than a styled look.

## Shopping Mall
**Program ratios** — GLA (gross leasable area) is typically ~60–70% of GBA; the balance is mall/common area (concourse, atria, public WCs, management suite), service corridors and back-of-house, plus plant. Common area is not waste — it's the whole product — but every extra metre of concourse is unlettable, so the ratio is the pro forma. Parking is usually a separate structure/area and its own math.
**Key metrics** — Anchor-to-anchor spacing along a mall run typically ~200–250 m maximum (walking-distance rule of thumb): beyond that, the pull collapses and the mid-run tenancies die. Classic "dumbbell" plan = anchor at each end, inline tenants between. Inline shop module commonly 6–8 m frontage × 20–30 m depth; mini-majors ~400–1,500 m²; anchors ~5,000–15,000 m²+. Mall concourse width typically ~8–12 m storefront-to-storefront (wider with kiosks or at atria). Retail rarely works above ~3 trading levels; every level up loses footfall unless vertical circulation is aggressive and visible. Food court seating ~1.4–1.9 m² per seat. Parking commonly ~4–5 spaces per 100 m² GLA (verify locally — it's a zoning number, not a design one).
**Code & compliance drivers** — Covered-mall provisions govern (IBC §402 in the US): a minimum mall width (20 ft / 6.1 m, with a substantial clear exit path within it), occupant load derived from GLA by formula rather than a flat factor, exit passageways from tenants, smoke control for atria, and travel-distance allowances that depend on the mall being kept clear. Fire compartmentation between tenancies and between mall and BOH. Every tenant needs egress that doesn't rely solely on the mall. Accessibility: lifts, ramps, accessible WCs, tactile/wayfinding. Kiosks and RMUs are regulated obstructions, not free floor — leasing will try to put them in your exit path.
**Spatial standards** — Storefront zone: a defined lease line with a controlled projection/blade-sign zone and a minimum clear height (commonly 3.0–4.5 m at the shopfront). Concourse as above, with clear width maintained past every kiosk. Service corridor 2.0–3.0 m wide with a rear door to *every* tenancy where possible; goods lifts and loading docks sized for the anchor's trucks, not the average one. Public WCs sized to catchment and located off the concourse but not on it. Escalator/lift landings need dwell space so they don't dump into circulation.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Anchor pulls shopper across the inline tenancies: entry → concourse → magnet at the far end, with the food court and cinema placed to pull traffic *up* to weak upper levels rather than sitting where footfall already is. Vertical circulation must be visible from the concourse and positioned to distribute, not concentrate. Voids/atria let upper-level tenants be *seen*; a solid slab between levels is a commercial decision to kill the upper floor. Service flow (deliveries, waste, tenant fit-out) runs on a separate loop behind the tenancies and never crosses the concourse.
**Common mistakes** — Only one anchor, or two anchors on the same side, so half the inline tenancies are on a dead run. Long straight concourse with no event/atrium punctuation. Dead corners and re-entrant angles that no tenant will lease. Too many levels. Service corridor treated as leftover space — then anchors can't take deliveries and shopfitters use the mall. Kiosks and RMUs added later, blocking sightlines to storefronts and narrowing the exit path. Escalators placed where they short-circuit the shopper past half the tenants. Designing the concourse as architecture and forgetting it's a shop window: if you can't see the storefronts, the storefronts don't trade.
**Render notes** — Malls need *crowds* — and crowds with variety: families, groups, someone sitting, someone carrying bags, a pram, a cleaner. Show tenant identity: distinct shopfronts, distinct signage, distinct interior lighting temperatures spilling into the concourse — a mall where every storefront is the same generic glass box reads instantly as CGI. Layer daylight from the atrium/skylight against warm tenant light. Include kiosks, seating, planting, wayfinding, bins, a coffee cart. Fake tells: an empty mall, blank white storefronts, no signage, no merchandising visible through the glass, a mirror-clean floor with no reflections of anything, and uniform lighting across concourse and shops.

## Showroom
**Program ratios** — Indicatively 55–75% display floor, 10–15% consultation/sales offices, 10–20% storage/prep/back-of-house, 5–10% customer amenity (café, WC, waiting). Automotive showrooms add a workshop/service wing that is often larger than the display hall and drives the whole site plan — treat it as a second building, not an annex.
**Key metrics** — Area per exhibit is the governing number. A car needs roughly 25–35 m² all-in (vehicle footprint ~4.8 × 2.4 m plus a 1.2–1.5 m walk-around and a viewing standoff); crowding vehicles is the fastest way to make a premium brand look cheap. Furniture vignettes 15–25 m²; kitchen/bathroom display bays 12–20 m². Ceiling 4.0–6.0 m for vehicles or large product (3.5 m+ for furniture) — a low ceiling flattens the product and the render both. Floor loading for vehicles and turntables must be checked (indicatively 5 kN/m² order of magnitude — confirm with the engineer). Lighting: ~500–1,000 lux general with accent contrast ~3:1 and CRI ≥ 90 — colour fidelity is the product here, and a poor-CRI source will get a paint or fabric sale rejected on the floor.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupancy classification is usually mercantile, but a vehicle display or a workshop brings additional requirements (fuel in vehicles, EV charging and battery storage, workshop as a separate hazard/compartment, ventilation and extraction, oil interceptors). Full-height glazed frontage triggers safety glazing, thermal/solar performance and — in most climates — a serious overheating and glare problem that the energy code will surface late if you ignore it. Accessible route to every display bay and consultation room; accessible WC. Vehicle entry door and internal manoeuvring must be code- and turning-circle-compliant.
**Spatial standards** — Vehicle entry door ~3.5 × 3.5 m minimum (check the largest model in the range); internal aisles for vehicle movement sized on the actual turning circle, not a guess. Customer circulation 1.5–1.8 m between displays; accessible clear width 915 mm minimum. Consultation pods/offices ~8–12 m² for a desk and 2–3 chairs, with acoustic separation — negotiating a purchase is a private act. Handover bay, if the brand has one, is a designed room, not a corner. Display plinths typically 100–200 mm; wall product displays within the 0.8–1.8 m sightline band.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Street/forecourt (the first display) → entry → hero exhibit visible immediately → a curated route past the range → consultation → handover/back-of-house. Consultation sits at the edge of the display floor with a view of the product but acoustic privacy from it. Delivery/prep flow (unload → valet/prep → display) must never cross the customer route, and for cars this means a service door and a prep bay from day one. Service/workshop customers need their own entrance and waiting area separate from the sales journey.
**Common mistakes** — The sun-blasted glass box: total transparency looks great in the render, then overheats, glares, fades the product and forces blinds that destroy the concept. No delivery/prep route, so new stock is driven across the customer floor. Ceiling too low, so the product reads squashed and the lighting can't be lifted off it. Overcrowded floor — more exhibits does not mean more sales. Generic spotlights with poor colour rendering, undoing the finish that the whole showroom exists to sell. Consultation desks out in the open, so customers won't commit. Ignoring the forecourt/frontage, which is the showroom that 100× more people see.
**Render notes** — Hero the product and light it like product photography: accent pools, a clean specular highlight on paint or lacquer, controlled reflections in the floor (a polished floor *should* reflect — just not like a perfect mirror). Show reflections in the glazing that read as a real environment, not a colour wash. Add people at a plausible density: a couple circling a car, a salesperson mid-gesture, a child. Include the unglamorous truth — a floor mat, a spec card, a price sheet, a coffee cup on the consultation table. Fake tells: uniformly bright box with no accenting, a mirror-perfect floor, no reflections on the product, a single lonely exhibit in a vast empty hall, and glazing that shows pure white outside instead of the forecourt and street the client actually bought.
