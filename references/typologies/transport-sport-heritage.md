# Typology Briefs — Transport, Sport, Exhibition & Heritage
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Crowd movement and egress in these typologies is life-safety
> critical and governed by specialist codes and, often, a licensing/safety authority. Nothing here
> substitutes for a fire engineer and a crowd-flow specialist. Heritage work is governed by statutory
> consent — never propose intervention without checking the listing/protection status.
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

Figures below are widely-published rules of thumb (Neufert, Architectural Graphic Standards, IATA ADRM-type
planning practice, the UK *Guide to Safety at Sports Grounds* "Green Guide", IBC/NFPA assembly provisions,
ICOMOS conservation charters). They vary by jurisdiction and by the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ).
Metric primary; imperial in parentheses where that unit dominates the discipline.

## Airport / Transport Hub

**Program ratios** — Terminal splits roughly **landside 35–45% / airside 45–55% / BOH-plant-offices 10–20%**
of terminal GFA (indicative; a low-cost pier-served terminal skews airside, a hub with large retail skews
airside further). Within airside, **hold rooms + gate lounges are typically the single largest block**, with
concessions commonly 5–10% of airside area in commercially-driven terminals. Processor areas (check-in,
security, immigration, baggage reclaim) plus their queues are the throughput-critical zone; circulation
(corridors, links, vertical) commonly runs **25–35% of gross**. Baggage handling (in-line screening, sortation,
make-up) is a large low-headroom-hungry BOH volume that planners chronically under-reserve.

**Key metrics** — Everything is sized on the **design peak hour** (typical planning basis: the busy-hour rate,
often the 30th–40th busiest hour of the year, **not** the annual peak). Derive: peak-hour departing pax,
peak-hour arriving pax, and the **peak 20-minute surge** at each processor. Processing rates (indicative,
verify with the operator): economy check-in desk ≈ **1.5–3 min/pax**; self-service kiosk ≈ **1–2 min/pax**;
a conventional security lane ≈ **~120–200 pax/hr**, higher with smart-lane/parallel divest; e-gate immigration
≈ **~20–30 s/pax**. Queue provision is set by a Level-of-Service target — planning practice allocates roughly
**1.0–1.8 m²/pax in queues** and **1.5–2.0 m²/pax in seated hold rooms** (indicative LOS "optimum" bands).
Aircraft stand mix and gate turn time drive hold-room size; a widebody hold room must seat a **single-aircraft
surge** (commonly 60–80% of seats-per-aircraft plus standing).

**Code & compliance drivers** — Egress and smoke control in very large, tall, connected volumes (performance-based
fire engineering is the norm, not prescriptive tables). **Security zoning is a hard architectural constraint**,
not a layer: sterile/airside, non-sterile/landside, restricted, and the one-way-valve boundaries between them
(arrivals must never merge into departures post-security; a single wrong door is a full terminal re-screen).
Border/customs zoning is a separate statutory regime layered on top. Accessibility to full step-free provision
throughout, including boarding. Fire compartmentation of baggage halls and fuel/plant. Blast/hostile-vehicle
mitigation at the forecourt is common on major hubs.

**Spatial standards** — Circulation width is sized on flow, not on gesture: Fruin/planning practice caps design
flow at roughly **~40–50 persons/min per metre of effective width** for level walkways at good LOS, and effective
width **excludes** boundary layers (~450 mm from walls, more from shopfronts, columns, seating and flight-info
displays — a 6 m corridor with retail both sides is not 6 m of flow). Main concourse spines on large terminals
commonly run **8–15 m clear**; piers **6–10 m**. Hold-room seat: **~0.5–0.6 m²/seat** plus circulation; benches
in rows with a **1.2 m+** aisle. Baggage reclaim: **linear metres of belt front** is the metric — allow a
frontage per arriving pax and a **deep standing zone (~5–8 m)** behind the belt for trolleys, or the hall jams.
Check-in island depth including queue commonly **10–14 m**. Trolley storage and lift/escalator capacity are
first-order, not afterthoughts.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Departures is a **one-way processing chain**: kerb → check-in/bag drop → security →
(emigration) → airside retail → hold room → gate → aircraft. Arrivals is the mirror: aircraft → (immigration) →
reclaim → customs → arrivals hall → kerb. The two must be **physically separated** across the security boundary
in section (two-level) or in plan (segregated corridors), with controlled one-way doors. **Walk distances** matter:
gate-to-security walks beyond roughly **~300 m** need travelators or people-mover; total kerb-to-gate should be
plannable within a stated maximum minutes budget. Retail wants a **post-security dwell** with the primary flow
passing through, not around it — but the flow must never be *forced* through in a way that blocks egress.

**Common mistakes** — Sizing on annual passengers instead of design peak hour. Forgetting that queues, not
counters, consume the floor: drawing 30 check-in desks with 4 m of queue in front of them. Ignoring boundary
layers so nominal corridor width is 30–40% wider than usable width. Letting arrivals and departures share a
corridor "for now". Under-reserving baggage-handling volume and then discovering the belt can't achieve its
gradient. Under-provisioning toilets at hold rooms and immigration (the two places people are trapped). Designing
a spectacular roof and then hanging the entire signage, lighting and services strategy off it as an afterthought.

**Render notes** — Convincing: **correct crowd density** — a peak-hour terminal is busy but not a mosh pit;
scattered clusters, queues with real spacing, trolleys, people seated with luggage between their feet, an
uneven distribution (dense at security, thin at the far pier). Signage and wayfinding present and legible.
The **scale of the long-span roof** read against a human figure. Glare-controlled daylight with visible
gradient across a deep plan. Aircraft tails outside as the scale anchor. Fake: an **empty terminal**, evenly
spaced identical people, no luggage, no signage, no queue, glassy floors with no scuff or reflection detail,
and a roof with no visible structural depth or services zone.

## Stadium & Sports Facility

**Program ratios** — Very roughly, of gross building area: **seating bowl (seats + gangways + vomitories) 40–50%**,
**concourses + toilets + concessions 20–30%**, **hospitality/premium 10–25%** (this is the swing variable and the
business model), **player/officials/media/BOH 10–15%**, plant/storage the remainder. Gross area per seat for a
modern stadium commonly lands in a broad **~1.0–2.0 m²/seat** band — premium-heavy venues sit at or above the top
of that; a simple bowl sits below it. Treat as indicative and confirm against comparables.

**Key metrics** — **Capacity** (and the licensed/safe capacity, which may be lower). **C-value** (sightline
quality: the vertical clearance a spectator has over the head of the person in front, to the design focal point):
**60 mm = the usual minimum acceptable, 90 mm = good, 120 mm = excellent (see over a standing spectator)**. The
focal point must be stated (touchline/pitch edge, not centre circle — the nearest point of action is the governing
one). **Evacuation time** is the other governing number: the Green Guide framework sets an evacuation time to a
place of safety of **2.5, 5 or 8 minutes** depending on fire-risk category, and normal egress from the bowl is
commonly targeted around **8 minutes**. **Rates of passage** (Green Guide, per **unit width of 550 mm**):
**~73 persons/min per unit width on stairways**, **~82 persons/min per unit width on level/gently-sloping routes**
— capacity of every gangway, vomitory, stair and gate is calculated from these, and the **lowest** link in the
chain sets the capacity of everything upstream.

**Code & compliance drivers** — Egress is the design, not a check on the design. Safety-authority licensing
(a safety certificate / capacity certificate regime in many jurisdictions) governs final capacity — the number
of seats you drew is not the capacity you get. Governing-body requirements (FIFA/UEFA/IOC/league) layer on:
pitch dimensions and run-offs, media and broadcast positions, doping control, mixed zone, floodlight levels.
Accessibility: wheelchair spaces distributed across price bands and **at multiple levels with companion seats and
enhanced sightlines** (a standing crowd in front is the classic failure). Fire compartmentation under stands.
Crowd segregation for away supporters where relevant. Structural: crowd-induced dynamic loading / synchronised
movement (grandstand vibration) is a real check, not a formality.

**Spatial standards** — Seat width **~460–500 mm** minimum (higher for premium); **row-to-row (tread) depth
~800–900 mm** for modern seated bowls, with a **minimum clearway of ~400 mm** between the back of one seat and the
front of the seat behind when tipped up. Gangways and stairs **min ~1.1 m** clear (and multiples of the 550 mm unit
width for capacity calculation). Handrails to every gangway. Vomitory width is derived from the seats it serves
divided by the rate of passage and the target evacuation time — **never chosen for its appearance**. Concourse:
plan a standing/queuing allowance in the order of **0.5–0.65 m²/person** for the design occupancy of that concourse
segment (indicative), and check it against the half-time surge, not the average. Toilets and food are sized on the
**15-minute half-time peak** — this is the number that gets stadiums torn apart in reviews.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Ingress is spread over **60–90 minutes** and can be metered by turnstiles; **egress is
a single simultaneous event** and cannot. Design for the "everyone leaves at once" condition and then check ingress
falls out of it. The chain is: seat → gangway → vomitory → concourse → stair/ramp → gate → **place of safety
outside the stadium boundary**, and then → the wider public realm and transport. That last leg is where stadiums
fail in practice: an egress route that discharges 40,000 people into a street that can't take them has not solved
egress, it has moved it. Player/officials, media, VIP and general public need **separate, non-crossing arrival
routes**. Broadcast compounds and pitch access for maintenance vehicles are large and get forgotten.

**Common mistakes** — **Designing the bowl geometry (or worse, the roof) before running sightlines.** A bowl that
looks right in section and fails C-value at row 3 must be rebuilt from the pitch outward. Setting the focal point
at the centre of the pitch instead of the nearest touchline. Undersizing egress for the simultaneous condition,
or sizing every element correctly except the one pinch point that throttles the whole route. Ignoring the
half-time surge on toilets/concessions/concourse. Placing wheelchair positions where a standing crowd blocks the
view. Forgetting the roof must not shade the pitch (turf grow-lighting is a cost the client will feel forever) and
must not put a shadow line across the broadcast camera's frame. Treating the concourse as leftover space under the
rake.

**Render notes** — Convincing: a **full bowl at plausible density**, with the crowd colour-massed (home end vs
away end), a *few* empty seats, and the crowd getting visually finer/greyer with distance rather than a repeating
stamped texture. The **long-span roof read as structure** — depth, node connections, a services and lighting zone.
Floodlight spill and a lit pitch against a dusk sky is the money shot for a reason. Human figures on the concourse
for scale. Fake: the **empty stadium** (instant CGI tell), a crowd of identical repeated figures, a pitch with no
mow pattern, a roof that appears to be a single thin surface, and a night render with no light source visible.

## Exhibition / Convention Centre

**Program ratios** — Of gross: **exhibition halls 50–65%**, **foyer/registration/circulation 15–25%**,
**meeting rooms + ballroom/plenary 10–20%**, **BOH (loading, storage, organiser offices, catering) 10–15%**.
Within a hall, **net saleable exhibit space is typically ~60–75% of gross hall area** (the rest is aisles,
columns-free-but-not-usable margins, service ducts). Foyer is commonly sized in the order of **20–30% of the
served hall area** — it is not a lobby, it is a **surge and registration machine**.

**Key metrics** — **Clear span** and **clear height** are the product being sold. Column-free spans of **30–60 m+**
are typical for major halls (the whole point of the typology is no columns). Clear height under structure commonly
**9–12 m+** in large halls (allowing tall stands, rigging and double-deck stands); anything under ~7 m limits what
can be sold. **Floor loading** is the other headline number: exhibition hall floors are commonly designed to
**~15–20 kN/m² (≈1,500–2,000 kg/m²)** to take forklifts, vehicles and heavy machinery — an order of magnitude above
a normal assembly floor. **Rigging capacity** from the roof (point loads on a grid) is now a commercial requirement.
Occupancy for egress calculation follows assembly factors: concentrated standing ≈ **0.65 m²/person (7 ft²)**,
less-concentrated seating ≈ **1.4 m²/person (15 ft²)**, exhibit floor ≈ **~3 m²/person (30 ft²) net** (IBC/NFPA
figures — confirm the applicable code).

**Code & compliance drivers** — Egress width from an assembly occupancy: the common IBC factors are **5.1 mm per
occupant for stairs** and **3.8 mm per occupant for level components** (0.3 in / 0.2 in) — for a 10,000-person hall
that is a very large number of very wide doors, and it must be **distributed** (remoteness of exits), not stacked
on one wall. Travel-distance limits from any point in a hall drive the exit spacing. Smoke control / smoke venting
in large volumes; sprinkler and detection strategy complicated by tall temporary stands and double-decks. Vehicle
access (a hall that admits cars/trucks is a different fire scenario). Accessibility across a very deep plan.

**Spatial standards** — Loading doors: commonly **~4–5 m wide × 4.5–5 m high** minimum to take an articulated
truck; loading docks at truck-bed height **plus** a level (at-grade) ramp entrance so vehicles can drive onto the
hall floor. **Hall floors should be flat, at grade, and directly truck-accessible** — every level change is a cost
paid every single show. Service pits/trenches on a grid (typically a regular module across the floor) for power,
data, water, drainage and compressed air. Main aisles inside a hall commonly **3 m+**; cross-aisles per organiser
layout. Hall subdivision by movable acoustic walls into smaller lettable units, each of which must independently
satisfy egress. Registration desk frontage and queuing space at the foyer sized for the **opening-morning surge**,
not the daily average.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Three flows that must not cross: **visitors** (street/transport → registration →
foyer → hall), **exhibitors and freight** (yard → dock → hall floor, during build-up and break-down), and
**catering/BOH**. Build-up/break-down is a heavy-vehicle industrial operation happening in the same room that will
host 10,000 people 12 hours later — the marshalling yard, dock count and truck stacking area are core program,
not a service afterthought. Foyers should serve **multiple halls** so halls can be combined or let separately.
Meeting rooms and ballroom want to be lettable **independently of the halls** (separate entry, separate egress,
separate catering route) — otherwise the venue can only run one event at a time.

**Common mistakes** — Under-speccing floor loading or rigging capacity and permanently limiting what the venue can
sell. Introducing columns "just a few" into a hall. Putting the hall on an upper level with no at-grade truck
access. Undersizing the marshalling yard so trucks queue on the public road. Designing a beautiful foyer that
cannot absorb the registration surge. Forgetting that every movable partition line needs its own compliant exit
set. Treating the ceiling as a finish rather than an exposed, accessible, load-bearing rigging and services zone.

**Render notes** — Convincing: **the sheer scale** — the hall read against forklifts, trucks, a rigged truss grid,
and small human figures far away in a deep plan. Exposed long-span structure with real depth and real connections.
A build-up render (half-erected stands, cherry-pickers, cable runs) is often more persuasive than a finished-show
render because it explains the building. Foyer scenes with plausible crowd density and daylight from a high
clerestory. Fake: an empty white void hall with no rigging, no services, no floor trenches, a ceiling that reads as
a flat plane, and a foyer with six people in a 3,000 m² space.

## Heritage Restoration

**Core principles (these govern everything below)** — **Record before you touch** (measured survey, photographic
record, materials and condition survey — the record is a deliverable in its own right and often a consent
condition). **Structural survey first** — never propose an intervention before you know what the fabric is doing.
**Minimum intervention** — do as much as necessary and as little as possible; repair in preference to replace.
**Reversibility** — a future generation must be able to undo your work without damaging the original fabric.
**Legibility of new vs old** — new work should be identifiable as new on close inspection (ICOMOS/Venice Charter
principle) while being subordinate and sympathetic; do not fake age, and do not create a false historical record.
**Like-for-like materials** — repair with matching, compatible materials (lime mortar to lime-built fabric, never
harder-than-the-stone cement pointing); compatibility matters more than strength. **Retain the maximum of
significant fabric.** Establish and write down the **statement of significance** — what makes this place matter —
because every subsequent decision is judged against it.

**Program ratios** — Program is a **residual** of what the fabric will accept, not an input imposed on it. Expect
significant "lost" area to structure, thick walls, non-compliant stairs and voids you cannot occupy. Net-to-gross
in a historic building is routinely far worse than in new-build — plan for it and warn the client early.

**Key metrics** — Condition-survey outputs, not planning ratios: moisture content, salt loading, mortar analysis,
timber decay/insect survey, structural movement (is it live or historic?), settlement monitoring over time.
Existing structural capacity (measured/tested, never assumed). Existing egress travel distances and stair widths
versus current standards, and the size of the gap.

**Code & compliance drivers** — **Statutory listing/protection status is the first thing you establish, before you
draw a line**: listed/scheduled/protected status, grade, curtilage, conservation area, world heritage buffer zone
— and the consent regime attached to each. Consent is required for works affecting **character**, including
internal work and including removal. Unauthorised works can be a criminal offence in many jurisdictions. Modern
fire, accessibility and energy requirements apply but are typically negotiated with the authority and the
conservation officer through a **balanced, justified case** — you argue for equivalence and compensatory measures
(sprinklers/detection instead of a widened stair; a platform lift instead of a demolished threshold), you do not
simply ignore them. Reference frameworks: Venice Charter, Burra Charter, Nara Document (authenticity), ICOMOS
principles; and where applicable the *Secretary of the Interior's Standards* (preservation / rehabilitation /
restoration / reconstruction — pick one and stay in it) or BS 7913-type guidance.

**Spatial standards** — Take them from **the building**, not from a book: existing floor-to-floor, existing window
reveals, existing stair geometry. New elements (lifts, risers, WCs, plant) should be inserted as **free-standing
pods within existing volumes**, touching the historic fabric as little as possible, and ideally reversibly.

**Flow & adjacency logic** — Route new services **without chasing historic fabric** — surface-mounted or in
existing redundant flues/voids, or in a new inserted core. New vertical circulation is easiest where fabric has
already been lost (a later extension, a bomb-damage repair, a stair that was already replaced in 1960).

**Common mistakes** — **Irreversible fixings** (resin anchors, chemical fixings, cement mortars, drilled fixings
into historic stone/timber) — the single most common and least forgivable failure. Cement pointing on soft
brick/stone (the fabric decays instead of the mortar). Sealing a breathable wall with impermeable modern
membranes, renders or paints and trapping moisture. Internal insulation and vapour barriers on solid walls without
a hygrothermal assessment — a well-meant retrofit that rots the wall plate. Stripping a building of later
accretions that are themselves significant. Starting design before the structural and condition survey. Assuming
consent is a formality. Copying original detailing so exactly that new and old cannot be distinguished (a false
historical record — the opposite failure to the crude modern insert, and equally wrong).

**Render notes** — Convincing: **weathering, patina and irregularity** — nothing is straight, nothing is uniform,
mortar joints vary, stone is differentially soiled and washed, glass is imperfect. New interventions rendered as
**unmistakably new**: clean, precise, materially distinct, and **visibly not touching** the old fabric (a shadow
gap is a conservation argument made visible). Show the junction. Fake: a "restored" building that reads as brand
new, uniformly clean masonry, perfectly straight lines, machine-perfect mortar, and a new insert pastiched into
the old so smoothly you cannot tell what is original — which is precisely the thing the charters forbid.

## Adaptive Reuse

**Core principles** — All of the heritage principles above apply whenever the host building is protected, and most
of them are simply good practice even when it isn't. Additionally: **the existing structure is the brief.** You are
not designing a building and then finding a shell for it; you are reading a shell and finding the program it will
carry. **Survey first, design second — always.**

**Program ratios** — Dictated by the **existing structural grid, floor-to-floor height and floor loading**. A
warehouse with a 6 m grid and 4.5 m floors will take almost anything; a 1960s office with 2.6 m floor-to-floor
will not become residential without losing the ceiling, and will not become a lab at all. Net-to-gross is worse
than new-build: expect **existing cores, thick walls, and unusable perimeter/plan residue**. Plan the new core
(lifts, risers, egress stair) early — it is usually the largest single move and it decides the plan.

**Key metrics** — The four that decide feasibility, in order: **(1) existing floor loading** (measured/assessed —
a historic timber office or residential floor frequently cannot carry an assembly, retail, library, plant or
archive load without strengthening); **(2) floor-to-floor and clear height after new services** (a 3.0 m
floor-to-floor minus 400–600 mm of new services and raised floor is a low room — model it before you promise it);
**(3) the structural grid** versus the new plan (car-park grids, warehouse grids and office grids each suit
different programs); **(4) façade performance** (existing glazing, thermal bridging, air-tightness) and whether it
can be upgraded within consent. Then: existing egress width and travel distance vs. the new occupancy; existing
foundation capacity if you add floors; the embodied-carbon saving (increasingly the reason the project exists —
quantify it).

**Code & compliance drivers** — Change of use usually triggers a **more onerous code regime** than the building was
built to: a change to assembly, residential or healthcare use typically demands more egress width, more exits,
compartmentation, sprinklers, acoustic separation, accessibility and energy performance. Existing-building code
provisions (and, in protected buildings, negotiated equivalence with the conservation authority) are the route
through. Structural: the building must be assessed against **current** loading codes for the **new** use — the fact
it has stood for 100 years is not an argument.

**Spatial standards** — Insert, don't carve. New cores, pods and mezzanines as **independent structures** that are
self-supporting and, ideally, demountable — this simultaneously solves reversibility, avoids loading the old
structure, and produces the legible new-vs-old reading the charters want. Where new floors are added inside a tall
volume, keep them **set back from the existing façade** so the original window rhythm is still read from inside and
out (slicing a floor plate across a tall window is the classic disfigurement).

**Flow & adjacency logic** — What the existing structure **will** allow: new openings in the direction of the span
(not across it), loads brought down on existing column lines, plant placed where the frame is strongest, cores
placed where fabric is already lost. What it **won't**: arbitrary new penetrations, heavy plant on a light floor,
a new stair through a primary beam, an atrium that removes the diaphragm holding the building square.

**Common mistakes** — Designing the scheme on the existing plan drawings and only then commissioning the
structural survey (the single most expensive mistake in the typology). Ignoring the structural grid and drawing a
plan that lands loads mid-span. Assuming the existing floor will take the new load. Forgetting the services zone
and delivering rooms with 2.2 m ceilings. Cutting an atrium without replacing the lost lateral stability.
Discovering asbestos/lead/contamination at construction stage. Using irreversible fixings into fabric you may want
back. Producing a scheme where the new work is indistinguishable from the old — or the opposite, where a strident
new insert overwhelms the significance of the host.

**Render notes** — Convincing: **the collision, honestly shown** — old fabric weathered, marked, patched, imperfect;
new work crisp, precise, materially distinct (steel, glass, exposed CLT, raw concrete), with **a visible joint or
shadow gap between them**. Retain evidence of the former life (crane rails, bolt scars, ghost marks of a demolished
partition, a painted sign) — these are the whole argument for the project. Daylight raking across an old surface
next to a new one. Fake: a scheme where the old building has been rendered as clean and uniform as the new insert;
new floor plates slicing across original windows with no acknowledgment; a "warehouse conversion" with no evidence
it was ever a warehouse.
