# Typology Briefs — Hospitality
> ⚠️ **Indicative standards, not code.** Figures are widely-used rules of thumb for design development —
> verify against local code, licensing authority and the operator's own brand standards (hotel operators
> impose their own key sizes, corridor widths and FF&E specs that override generic guidance).
> **Preservation Contract applies** (see `SKILL.md` §2).

---

## Hotel
**Program ratios** — Guestroom block (keys + corridors + floor service) typically 55–70% of GFA; public/FOH (lobby, F&B, meeting, spa, retail) 10–25%; BOH (kitchens, laundry, stores, plant, staff, admin, loading) 15–30%. The ratio is the business model: a limited-service hotel is ~75–85% guestroom block with a token lobby; a full-service/convention hotel can drop below 55% because ballroom + kitchen + BOH swell. Efficiency (net rentable / gross) usually lands 60–70%.
**Key metrics** — GFA per key: budget/limited-service 30–45 m²; midscale 45–65 m²; upscale/full-service 65–95 m²; luxury 100–150+ m² (all indicative). Guestroom net area: budget 18–24 m²; midscale 25–32 m²; upscale 32–42 m²; luxury/suites 45 m²+. Standard US room module ≈ 3.6–4.2 m (12–14 ft) bay × 7.5–9.0 m deep. Bathroom 4.5–7 m². Guest lifts: ~1 per 70–100 keys (rule of thumb; verify with traffic analysis). Parking 0.3–1.2 spaces/key by location.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Two remote means of egress from every guestroom floor; sprinklered travel distance and dead-end corridor limits govern the length of a double-loaded slab. Accessible keys typically a fixed percentage of inventory (e.g. ADA scales roughly 2–4% with a share roll-in shower) — check the local figure, and distribute them across room types, not all in one tier. Fire-rated corridor and guestroom demising construction; smoke-control at atria. Commercial kitchen extraction, grease duct rating and shaft routing to roof. Acoustics: guestroom party walls and floor/ceiling assemblies need a serious rating (operators commonly demand STC/Rw ≈ 50–55+ and impact isolation) — plumbing walls, lift shafts and back-of-house are the usual failures.
**Spatial standards** — Guest corridor clear width 1.5–1.8 m (brand standards often exceed code minima); ceiling 2.4–2.7 m. Room clear ceiling 2.6 m+ preferred after ductwork. Bed clearance: ≥0.75 m one side of a double, 0.9–1.2 m circulation at foot of bed. Bathroom door swing must not foul the WC. Housekeeping/linen store on every guest floor (~8–15 m²). Service lift + refuse route to every floor.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Four flows, kept apart: guest (porte-cochère → lobby → registration → vertical core → key), staff (separate entry, timeclock, lockers, canteen → BOH corridor), goods (loading dock → receiving → stores → kitchen), refuse/soiled (kitchen + laundry → dock, never back through a guest corridor). BOH corridor must reach every FOH point (banqueting, restaurant, bar, pool) via service doors that guests never see. Never route a service lift to open into a guest corridor with a swing door in view of the lobby. Kitchen adjacent to the ballroom/banquet at the same level, with a pantry/staging buffer for noise.
**Common mistakes** — Designing the guestroom last and forcing it into a structural grid that yields 3.2 m bays (unrentable). Putting the lift lobby where it forces long dead-end corridors. Sizing the BOH by leftover area — under-sized loading, no staff canteen, no laundry, no chair store for the ballroom (a 400-seat ballroom needs a stacked-chair/table store of real size). Forgetting the porte-cochère height for coaches. Placing plant/lift machinery above a top-floor suite. Treating accessible rooms as an afterthought and clustering them on one floor.
**Render notes** — Warm interiors 2700–3000K with layered sources (table lamps, cove, wall sconces, art accents) and a visible luminance hierarchy — a hotel lobby lit by one uniform ceiling wash reads as an office. Dusk exterior at ~15–20 min after sunset, warm windows against a deep blue sky, lit porte-cochère, one or two cars/valet. Interiors need occupancy and residue of use: a guest at reception, luggage, an open laptop at the lounge, rumpled cushion, a drink on the bar. Sterile giveaways: empty lobbies, perfectly aligned furniture, no reflections in polished stone, no soft goods (curtains, throws, rugs), plants with no soil.

## Resort
**Program ratios** — Keys are dispersed (villas/pavilions/low-rise blocks); the plan is land-driven. Typical split: accommodation 40–60% of built GFA; public/amenity (F&B, spa, pool, kids, retail, arrival) 20–30%; BOH + staff (incl. staff housing, often a village of its own) 20–30%. Built footprint is frequently a small share of the site (site coverage often 10–25%) — the landscape *is* the program.
**Key metrics** — GFA per key: 80–150 m² (resort rooms/suites) up to 200–350 m² for pool villas (indicative). Density often 15–40 keys/ha for low-rise resorts. F&B seats: roughly 1.0–1.5 seats/key across all outlets (more if the resort chases external covers). Pool: sun loungers ≥1 per key at a beach/pool resort; allow ~4–6 m² of deck per lounger. Spa 1–2 m² per key; treatment room 16–25 m² (couple's suite 30–40 m²). Comfortable guest walking distance to the main facilities ≤ 400–500 m, else buggy transport is mandatory.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Coastal/shoreline setback lines and environmental permits usually dominate. Flood/storm-surge levels drive finished floor levels. Pool: barrier/fencing, depth marking, plant room, water treatment. Accessible route across a sand/gravel/dune landscape (this is where resorts fail accessibility). Fire access: emergency vehicle route within reach of every villa cluster, and a water main that reaches the far end of the site. Wastewater/STP and potable water for a site typically off-grid.
**Spatial standards** — Villa: bedroom + bathroom + terrace/deck (deck depth ≥ 2.4 m to be usable with a lounger + table); private pool commonly 3 × 6 m to 4 × 8 m. Villa-to-villa privacy: control sightlines with level change, wall or planting, not distance alone. Buggy path 2.4–3.0 m; service path separate, 3.0–3.5 m for a small truck. Guest path gradient ≤ 1:20 where it is the accessible route.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — The whole resort is a BOH/FOH problem stretched over hectares. A back-of-house spine (service road or tunnel) must reach every villa cluster, restaurant and the beach club for F&B runners, housekeeping, laundry and refuse — without ever crossing a guest path or being visible from a terrace. Staff village, laundry, main kitchen, stores and generator sit on the "back" of the site, usually near the service entry, screened. Arrival sequence: reveal-and-conceal — the reception should frame the primary view (sea/valley), not the car park. Kids' club noise away from spa and adult pool.
**Common mistakes** — Designing the villa plan without the service route, so housekeeping carts trundle along the guest boardwalk. Orienting every villa at the same angle so each one looks into the next one's pool. Ignoring the sun path — west-facing glass in the tropics = an unusable afternoon room. Forgetting staff accommodation and transport entirely (it is 20–30% of the built area and it does not fit later). Beach pavilion with no back-of-house pantry, so drinks come 200 m from the main kitchen. Under-sizing the STP, generator and desalination compound and then hiding them badly.
**Render notes** — Golden hour or blue-hour lit-pool shots; warm 2700K lamps + candles read against cool ambient. Water needs a story: gentle ripples, caustics, a slightly wet deck edge, wind on the palms. People at low density and mid-distance (a couple on the deck, a staff member with a tray) — resort renders die from being empty. Styling: towels rolled on loungers, a book face-down, half-drunk glass, sarong on a chair, footprints in the sand. Fake tells: mirror-flat pool water, identical CG palms in a grid, hyper-saturated turquoise, no shade structure in a tropical sun, immaculate sand.

## Restaurant
**Program ratios** — Classic split for a full-service restaurant: dining/front-of-house ≈ 55–65% of GFA, kitchen + prep + dish ≈ 20–30%, storage/receiving/refuse/staff/WCs ≈ 10–20%. A useful rule: kitchen and its support ≈ 40–50% of the dining area. Fine dining pushes kitchen and service space up; fast-casual pushes it down. Bar (if any) 5–15% of FOH.
**Key metrics** — Area per cover (dining room only, indicative): cafeteria/fast-casual 1.1–1.4 m²; casual full-service 1.4–1.7 m²; upscale/fine dining 1.8–2.3 m²; banquet/function 1.0–1.3 m². Table sizes: 2-top 600–750 mm square/round; 4-top ≈ 900 × 900 mm; round 6-top ø 1.2–1.4 m. Place setting 600 mm wide × 400 mm deep. Server station roughly one per 20–40 covers. Occupant-load factor for the dining area is far denser than the design layout (IBC ≈ 1.4 m² / 15 sf net per person) — size egress on that, not on your table plan.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load usually forces two exits well before the room "feels" big; exit width sizing and door swing in the direction of travel. Accessible route to a percentage of tables, accessible WC, counter section at ≤ 865 mm. Kitchen: Type I grease hood over all cooking equipment with an overhang beyond the appliance line, grease duct with fire-rated enclosure to roof, fire suppression (wet chemical) interlocked to fuel shut-off, and mechanical make-up air (broadly ~80% of exhaust — the balance is a designed number, get it from the MEP). Grease interceptor on kitchen waste. Separate hand-wash sinks; food-safe finishes (coved, washable). Acoustic and odour separation from any residential above.
**Spatial standards** — Main circulation aisle 1.2–1.5 m; service aisle between chair backs 1.0–1.4 m; back-of-chair to back-of-chair across a service aisle 1.35–1.5 m. Chair pull-out 450–600 mm behind the table edge. Kitchen aisles: 1.0–1.2 m single-sided, 1.4–1.6 m where two staff pass back-to-back or hot equipment faces prep. Pass/expo counter length matched to the number of plated courses in flight. Ceiling ≥ 2.7 m in the kitchen after hood and ductwork.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — One-way food logic: receiving → dry/cold store → prep → cook line → pass/expo → dining. Dirty logic runs the other way and must never re-cross clean: bussing → dish drop → wash → clean store. Guests never see the dish pit; servers never carry plates through the dish return. Separate entry, WCs and lockers for staff; refuse route from the kitchen to the bin store without crossing the dining room or the entrance. Host stand controls the room from the door with a sightline to the whole floor. Bar within a few steps of the pass if drinks are runner-delivered.
**Common mistakes** — Sizing the kitchen from what's left over, then discovering the hood, the walk-in and the dish machine don't fit. Forgetting the make-up air and creating a negative-pressure room that slams the front door and sucks in street noise. Designing an elegant open kitchen and then routing the dish return past it. Ignoring the WC count and the accessible WC until the plan is fixed. Under-sizing dry/cold storage and refuse (bins for a busy restaurant are a room, not a corner). Banquette-only layouts that give no flexibility for party size. No dwell/waiting zone at the door, so the queue stands in the dining aisle.
**Render notes** — Dining rooms want warm, low, punctuated light: 2400–2900K, pools of light on the tables (pendant/downlight aimed at the tabletop, not the aisle), dim ambient, glare-free. Contrast is the point — a uniformly bright restaurant render reads as a canteen. Include people mid-action (mid-conversation, a server walking with slight motion blur), half-eaten plates, wine at different levels, crumpled napkins, one pushed-out chair. Fake tells: every chair perfectly tucked, untouched place settings, no drinks, no menus, glassware without smudges or reflections, a spotless open kitchen with no staff in it.

## Café
**Program ratios** — Seating/FOH 50–65% of GFA; service counter + bar-back + barista zone 15–25%; kitchen/prep (if food) 10–20%; storage/staff/WC/refuse the balance. A pure espresso bar can invert this and be nearly all counter. Queue and order zone must be programmed, not left to chance.
**Key metrics** — Area per cover 1.1–1.6 m² (café/coffee-shop seating; tighter than a restaurant). Table: 2-top 600–700 mm square; communal table 750–900 mm deep, allow 600 mm per person. Barista workstation: 1.8–2.4 m of linear counter per barista (indicative); espresso machine + grinder + knock-box + steam station in that run. Queue allowance ≈ 0.5–0.8 m² per waiting person; plan for 4–8 people at peak minimum. Counter height 900–1050 mm (service) / 1100 mm bar with 250–300 mm overhang for stools.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load counts standing/queue area too. Accessible portion of the service counter ≤ 865 mm high with knee clearance if it's an order/consult point. Hand-wash sink and food-safe finishes behind the counter. Espresso machines need dedicated power (often a 3-phase or a high-amp dedicated circuit), softened water supply and a drain — these are architectural, not "coordinated later". Extraction only if there's cooking; a toaster/panini line may still need a Type II condensate hood. Grease interceptor if there's a fryer or griddle.
**Spatial standards** — Behind-counter working aisle 900–1200 mm (below 900 mm, two staff can't pass). Counter working depth 750–900 mm. Order point → pickup point should be separated by 1.5–3.0 m so the queue doesn't block collection. Circulation aisle in the seating 900–1200 mm; 1.5 m where it doubles as the accessible route. Ceiling and soft surfaces matter: hard-surface cafés hit 75–85 dB(A) at peak and become unusable for the laptop crowd.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Linear guest flow: door → queue (parallel to the counter, not across the door) → order/pay → pickup → condiment station → seat. Pickup and condiments must be off the queue line. Staff loop behind the counter is a closed working island with its own route to the store, dishwash and bin — a barista should never have to walk through the seating to reach the back. Deliveries (milk daily, beans weekly) and refuse need a route that doesn't cross the queue at 8am.
**Common mistakes** — Putting the queue where it collides with the entrance door swing and with the pickup point. Counter too shallow, so the espresso machine's back-of-machine service access is inside a wall. No dedicated milk-fridge under-counter, so staff fetch from the back all day. Ignoring the drain and water softener. Designing every seat as a laptop seat (kills turnover) or none (kills the daytime trade) — the seat mix should be intentional: bar/window seats, 2-tops, one communal table, a lounge corner. No acoustic absorption at all.
**Render notes** — Daylight-driven: big soft window light, 5000–5600K daylight balanced against 2700–3000K pendants over the counter; that mix is what reads as "café". Show the machine in use — steam wisp, portafilter on the counter, cups stacked, a chalkboard with real handwriting, pastries in a case, a tiny mess (spilled beans, a used cloth). One or two people at the counter, someone seated with a laptop and a cup. Fake tells: an empty counter with no equipment, no cups, no menu, no crumbs; a perfectly clean chalkboard; plants floating over invisible soil; uniformly bright white ambient light.

## Bar / Nightlife
**Program ratios** — For a bar: bar + back-bar + service well 15–25% of GFA; standing/seating floor 45–60%; BOH (cellar/keg store, ice, glasswash, spirits store, staff, refuse) 15–25%; WCs are disproportionately large in nightlife and must be programmed early. For a nightclub, add a dance floor (20–35% of FOH) and DJ/AV/control zone; seating shrinks and standing dominates.
**Key metrics** — Bar length per bartender: 1.8–2.4 m (6–8 ft) of serving bar, with one service well per one or two bartenders (indicative). Standing occupancy ≈ 0.5–0.7 m² per person (IBC standing factor ≈ 0.46 m² / 5 sf net — always check the local load factor, it can be denser than your layout). Dance floor ≈ 0.5–0.8 m² per dancer. Bar die: 600–700 mm deep, 1050–1150 mm high; stool seat 750 mm, one stool per 600 mm of bar. Back-bar aisle 900–1100 mm clear. Cocktail table ø 600–700 mm.
**Code & compliance drivers** — Occupant load is the design driver: a standing-density venue reaches multi-hundred occupancy in a small footprint, which forces multiple exits, egress width (sized per person), panic hardware, and an exit capacity that often can't be squeezed through the front door. Emergency lighting and illuminated exit signage that survive a dark, smoky room. Licensing: capacity certificate, hours, dispersal/queue management outside, sometimes a sound limiter. Noise to neighbours is the classic killer: internal levels commonly 95–100+ dB(A) with heavy low-frequency content; you need mass, isolation and a sound-lock lobby (two doors, never one). Structure-borne bass needs isolated floating floors / resilient hangers, not just acoustic plasterboard. Ventilation for a dense standing crowd (heat + humidity) is a real load.
**Spatial standards** — Clear zone in front of the bar 1.5–2.0 m for a two-deep standing crowd + a circulation lane behind it (≥1.2 m). Bar top width 600 mm minimum to land a drink and lean. Service well contains ice, speed rail, soda gun, glass rack — do not "value engineer" it out. Glasswash and keg cellar within a short pull of the bar (long beer lines = warm, foaming beer; keep the run short and chilled). DJ booth with sightline to the floor, cable route and its own power. WCs sized for real peak use, with queue space that is not in the escape route.
**Flow & adjacency logic** — Entry sequence: queue → door/ID → cloakroom → sound lock → room. Never let the front door open directly into the room (light, noise, cold, and it breaks the licence). Guest flow must not cross the bartender's service loop or the glass/keg route from the cellar. Bottle and keg deliveries plus glass/bottle refuse are heavy, frequent and loud — give them a back door and a store, or they will happen through the front at 10am and the bins will sit on the street. Security sightlines: a clear view from the door to the bar and the WC corridor.
**Common mistakes** — Designing a beautiful long bar with nowhere for the bartender to stand, no service well and no under-counter refrigeration. One door as both entrance and sole exit for a 300-person occupant load. No sound lock, so the licence dies in month two. Forgetting the keg cellar/line run and the ice machine (ice is a plant item, not an appliance). Under-sizing WCs and letting the queue eat the fire route. Treating the dance floor as leftover space with a column in the middle of it. Hard surfaces everywhere plus a big PA = an unintelligible, painful room.
**Render notes** — Nightlife renders live or die on light: strong colour (deep amber, magenta, cyan), high contrast, practical sources IN frame — back-bar bottle lighting, neon/LED strip, candle, pendant over the bar, a haze/atmospheric volume so beams read. Expose for the highlights and let the crowd fall into silhouette; low camera height, some motion blur on people. Styling: bottles two-deep on the back bar, glassware, a cocktail mid-build, coats on stool backs, condensation, a wet bar top. Fake tells: even white light, an empty bar with no bottles and no bartender, spotless surfaces, a legible-in-every-corner exposure, no haze so the beams don't exist, and a dance floor with nobody on it.
